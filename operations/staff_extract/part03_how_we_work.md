# PART 3 — HOW WE WORK HERE

This section defines the operating system of this facility. How tasks work, how authority works, and what happens when things go wrong.

---

## The Three Questions

Before you do anything, ask yourself:

1. **Was it assigned?**
2. **Is it written?**
3. **Was it approved?**

If the answer to all three is no — you do not do it. You stop and you ask.

This is not about slowing you down. It is about preventing the damage that happens when people improvise. Every time someone acts without authorization, something breaks — diets get changed, exhibits get modified, products get ordered, systems get introduced. This rule exists to prevent all of that.

---

## The Claim-Based Work System

This is how tasks work here:

1. **A task is posted.** It appears on the task board, in a message, or from a manager.
2. **You claim it.** You say: "I will take this." That means it is now yours.
3. **You do it.** You execute the task according to the written procedure.
4. **It gets verified.** A manager confirms it was done correctly.

If you do not claim it, it is not yours. If nobody claims it, we have a problem — and that problem gets escalated immediately.

**What this replaces:** The old system where tasks floated around and nobody was accountable. The old system where people said "I thought someone else was doing that." That is over. Every task has a name on it.

---

## The Authorization Model

**Who can approve what:**

- **Owner:** Sets direction, standards, and policy. Final authority on system changes, exhibit design, diet formulas, and policy changes.
- **Upper Managers:** Approve all purchases (credit card holders limited to $350 max). Approve vendor contracts. Approve animal moves and vet visits. Identify issues BEFORE they become problems — notify the owner for decisions. Examples: animal could get hurt in current exhibit setup, browse supply running low by Thursday — order now.
- **Staff:** Execute claimed tasks within existing procedures. Cannot approve anything. If something requires approval, escalate to a manager. If the manager cannot approve it, it goes to the owner.

**The rule is simple:** If you are about to do something that costs money, changes a system, or modifies how we operate — stop and get approval first.

---

## The Escalation Ladder

When something goes wrong, here is what happens:

- **Strike 1: Correction.** You made a mistake. You are told what you did wrong and how to fix it. Everyone makes mistakes. This is normal.
- **Strike 2: Formal warning + retraining.** You made the same mistake again, or you made a serious error. You receive a formal warning. You are retrained on the correct procedure. This goes in your file.
- **Strike 3: Disciplinary action.** You have been corrected and warned. The behavior continues. At this point, disciplinary action is taken. This may include suspension, reassignment, or termination.

**This applies to everyone.** Staff, managers, everyone. Animal welfare overrides convenience, and accountability overrides seniority.

**The focus is on correcting behavior, not punishing people.** But if correction does not work, escalation is required.

## Technology and Software Governance

**Rule: No software, app, or tracking system is adopted without owner written approval.**

This rule exists because unauthorized software adoption caused significant operational damage in the past. That will not happen again.

Here is how it works now:

- No apps for task management without owner approval.
- No scheduling software without owner approval.
- No diet or feeding software without owner approval.
- No communication platforms (beyond what we already use) without owner approval.
- If a vendor offers a tool, the answer is NO until the owner evaluates it.
- If a coworker suggests a new app, the answer is NO until the owner approves it.
- The only systems we use are the ones the owner has approved.

**If you are currently using any software that was not explicitly approved by the owner, stop using it and report it.**

---

## Purchasing Authority

**Rule: No purchases without upper manager approval.**

- Only upper managers or owner-designated individuals can order product.
- Only the owner can approve new vendors.
- All purchases require documentation (what was ordered, from whom, how much, why).
- Budget thresholds that require additional approval are set by the owner.
- No new vendors without owner sign-off.
- Credit card holders have a $350 maximum. Anything above that goes to the owner.

**Why this rule exists:** Staff were ordering 60 bags of produce every few days. Staff were purchasing 32 varieties of specialized diet from Missouri. None of this was approved by the owner. The purchasing system had no controls. That is over.

## Miranda Rights Doctrine

Before any correction or escalation, three questions must be answered:

1. What constraint existed?
2. Was the action discretionary or required?
3. Is this a one-time situation or a repeated pattern?

If any answer is unclear, escalation pauses. No public correction without clarification. No attributing intent without verification. No assuming motivation from outcome alone.

**Core statement: Observe first. Clarify constraints. Then decide.**
