DROMEDARY CAMEL
*Camelus dromedarius*

Order: Artiodactyla
Native to: Arabian Peninsula / North Africa
Diet: Herbivore (thorny plants, dry grasses, browse)
Respiration: Lungs

1. What does it see?
Camels have a wide lateral visual field due to eye placement on the sides of the head, supplemented by a third eyelid (nictitating membrane) that is transparent enough to allow vision during sandstorms while protecting the corneal surface. Long double-row eyelashes reduce glare from bright reflective desert surfaces and physically block windblown sand from reaching the eye.

2. What does it sense?
The camel's nostrils can seal completely — closing to a narrow slit — during sandstorms to exclude particulates while still allowing passive gas exchange through the nasal mucosa. Olfaction is the primary tool for evaluating forage quality and reproductive state; camels perform flehmen (curling the upper lip and drawing air across the vomeronasal organ) to read chemical signals from urine, dung, and secretions.

3. How does it move?
Camels are pacers: both legs on the same side move forward together, producing a rolling gait that is energetically efficient for sustained travel over loose sand and rocky desert substrate. The two broad, leathery toes spread under body weight to distribute load across a wide surface area, preventing the foot from sinking into soft sand.

4. How does it breathe?
Camel nasal passages are structurally convoluted (turbinate-rich), recapturing up to 66% of the moisture from exhaled air before it exits the nostril — a critical mechanism for reducing respiratory water loss in arid environments. Resting metabolic rate is slightly lower than expected for body size, reducing both water loss and heat production during periods of inactivity in heat.

5. What fuels it?
The hump stores fat — not water — as a concentrated energy reserve that can be metabolized during food scarcity; fat catabolism also produces metabolic water as a byproduct of oxidation. Thorny plants, dry browse, and salt-tolerant succulents that other ungulates avoid form the dietary base; camels can consume halophytic plants with salt concentrations toxic to most mammals and excrete excess salt through highly concentrated urine.

6. What limits it?
Prolonged water deprivation beyond body condition loss of ~25% of body mass begins to impair renal function; camels can tolerate this level of dehydration and rehydrate rapidly (consuming up to 135 liters in minutes) but cannot survive indefinitely without water access. Hump depletion from extended food deprivation causes the hump to soften and lean to the side, indicating negative energy balance that impairs thermoregulation and locomotor performance.

7. What decision is constant?
When does the energy cost of walking to the next known water source become less than the cost of remaining in the current location without water? When does consuming a salt-laden plant represent a net gain or loss given the additional water required to process and excrete the salt load?

8. What role does it play?
In arid ecosystems, camels are one of few large herbivores capable of processing thorny, xeric, and salt-tolerant vegetation that remains unavailable to other grazers, and their selective consumption helps shape the structure of arid plant communities. Dung deposits in desert environments provide organic matter that supports specialized invertebrate and microbial communities in otherwise nutrient-sparse soils.
