NORTH AMERICAN RIVER OTTER
*Lontra canadensis*

Order: Carnivora
Native to: North America
Diet: Carnivore (fish, crayfish, amphibians)
Respiration: Lungs

1. What does it see?
River otters have eyes adapted for both aerial and underwater use; a flat cornea reduces the optical distortion that occurs when light transitions from water to the lens, providing functional acuity in both media. Underwater vision is sufficient to track actively moving fish at close range, and the otter's pupils constrict to a point in bright surface light to reduce glare during surface scanning.

2. What does it sense?
Whiskers (vibrissae) are long, thick, and highly innervated; they detect water pressure waves generated by the movement of fish or crayfish in murky or dark water, functioning as a hydrodynamic detection system that operates independently of vision. Scent marking with anal gland secretions deposited at latrines on the water's edge communicates individual identity, territory use, and reproductive condition to other otters moving through the area.

3. How does it move?
In water, the otter propels itself primarily by dorsoventral undulation of the spine and tail, with the webbed hind feet functioning as rudders during turns; forelimbs are held against the body during fast swimming. On land, otters move with a bounding gait using all four limbs, often tobogganing on snow or mud banks by stretching the body flat and sliding — a behavior that also reduces the energy cost of downslope travel.

4. How does it breathe?
As a fully air-breathing mammal, the otter surfaces regularly during foraging dives; typical dives last 30–60 seconds, with a physiological capacity for dives extending to several minutes. During submersion, heart rate drops through a diving reflex that reduces peripheral circulation and conserves oxygen for the brain and heart — a mechanism shared across diving mammals and birds.

5. What fuels it?
Fish are the primary prey, seized with the teeth after an underwater pursuit and consumed at the surface or on a bank; crayfish and amphibians supplement the diet seasonally, particularly when fish are less available in winter-cold water or during breeding aggregations. One to several whole fish or an equivalent portion of fish pieces alongside crayfish constitutes a daily ration; prey size is calibrated to what can be captured and processed efficiently without tools.

6. What limits it?
Water quality is a direct constraint: otter population density tracks fish abundance and water clarity, and chemical contamination that reduces prey populations removes the energy base that supports otter territories. Fur must be kept clean and groomed; the insulating capacity of otter fur depends on a layer of trapped air within the dense underfur, which is lost if the fur becomes contaminated with oil or sediment, rapidly leading to hypothermia in cold water.

7. What decision is constant?
When does the energetic return from pursuing a fleeing fish through open water exceed the cost of the chase, versus abandoning and searching for a less mobile prey item in substrate cover? When does the current latrine site carry enough chemical information about recent otter activity to make extended investigation worthwhile before moving on?

8. What role does it play?
River otters are apex predators within stream and lake food webs; their selective predation on abundant, easily caught fish species (often slower or injured individuals) creates pressure that shapes fish population age structure and behavior. As consumers of crayfish, otters regulate crayfish population density, indirectly influencing the aquatic vegetation and invertebrate communities that crayfish overgraze when uncontrolled.
