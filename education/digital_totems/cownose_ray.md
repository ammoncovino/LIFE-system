## Screen 2 — Quick Learn

**What can it see, hear, or feel?**
Cownose rays have a sense humans do not have at all: they can detect tiny electrical fields produced by living things. This lets them find clams and oysters buried in sand where there is nothing to see or smell. They also use smell, pressure sensing through their lateral line system, and vision.

**Where does it live — and why?**
It lives in the western Atlantic Ocean and Gulf of Mexico, from shallow coastal bays to open water. It migrates seasonally — moving north in spring and south in fall — following water temperature and food availability. It needs sandy or muddy bottom habitat where buried shellfish are accessible.

**How does it send messages?**
Rays communicate primarily through posture and proximity within the school. They produce low-frequency pressure waves through movement that can be detected by nearby individuals. Chemical signals in the water may also carry reproductive information during breeding season.

**What does it do when something changes?**
In a school, one individual's change in direction rapidly spreads through the group — the school responds as a coordinated unit without a designated leader. Individually, they may accelerate and change direction to avoid large objects or sharp stimuli.

**How does it learn?**
Rays can learn through experience — they remember locations of productive foraging areas and return to them. Young rays travel with adults, which may expose them to successful feeding locations and seasonal movement routes.

**How does it have babies?**
Cownose rays are live-bearing. A female carries one pup internally for about 11–12 months, nourishing it through a placenta-like structure. The pup is born fully formed and immediately capable of independent movement.

**What are its limits?**
It depends on water temperature within a specific range — cold water triggers migration. It requires sandy or muddy bottom habitat for feeding. Its hard tooth plates work on shellfish but not on all food types. Large shark species are its primary predators.

**What does this teach us?**
An animal can sense an entire category of information — electrical fields — that is completely absent from human experience. The world contains more information than any one type of sensor can detect.

## Screen 3 — Go Deeper

**Perception**
Rhinoptera bonasus possesses electroreception through a network of specialized sense organs called the ampullae of Lorenzini — gel-filled pores concentrated around the snout that detect voltage gradients as small as a few nanovolts per centimeter. This system detects the bioelectric fields produced by the muscle contractions and nerve activity of prey animals buried in sediment, where visual or olfactory detection would be ineffective. The lateral line system — mechanoreceptors along the body surface — detects water displacement and pressure gradients produced by nearby moving objects. Vision is functional but the eyes are positioned laterally and dorsally, suggesting a wide-field threat-detection role rather than precision prey location. Olfaction is well-developed and likely important for detecting chemical gradients associated with prey and conspecifics.

**Environment**
The cownose ray inhabits the western Atlantic from southern New England to Brazil, including the Gulf of Mexico and Caribbean Sea. It occupies a range of marine environments from shallow estuaries and bays to offshore open water during migration. It is strongly associated with benthic (bottom) habitat that contains populations of bivalves — clams, oysters, quahogs, and similar mollusks — which constitute the primary diet. Seasonal migration patterns are well-documented: large schools move northward along the US Atlantic coast in spring and return south in fall, tracking preferred water temperatures between approximately 15 and 30 degrees Celsius. Schools of thousands travel together during peak migration periods.

**Signal**
Social communication in cownose rays is not as well-characterized as in vocally communicating species. Within schools, hydrodynamic signaling — pressure waves and water movement produced by the swimming of neighboring individuals — likely coordinates turning and speed changes. During breeding season, males follow females closely, tracking chemical signals likely released in the water. Courtship behavior includes males biting the female's pectoral fin — a physical contact signal that initiates mating. The school itself, as an aggregation, functions as a collective signal: a school of thousands is harder for predators to target effectively than isolated individuals.

**Response**
Individual responses to threat are fast but the school-level response is emergent and coordinated. When one individual in a school changes speed or direction sharply, neighbors respond within milliseconds — a cascade that propagates through the school without each individual necessarily having detected the original stimulus. This produces the characteristic wave-like turning behavior seen in large schools of fish and rays. Individual rays may also breach — leaping partially or fully out of the water — during large-school migration events, the function of which remains unclear but may relate to parasite removal or social signaling.

**Learning**
Spatial learning in elasmobranch (shark and ray) species has been demonstrated in laboratory settings — individuals can learn to associate specific locations or stimuli with food rewards and retain this information across time. In wild cownose rays, familiarity with seasonal migration routes and productive foraging areas likely develops through experience combined with following conspecifics. Young rays travel within large schools that include experienced adults, providing potential for observational acquisition of route and habitat knowledge.

**Reproduction**
Cownose rays have a viviparous reproductive strategy with placental analogue nutrition — the single pup developing internally is nourished by secretions from the uterine wall after yolk reserves are depleted. Gestation is approximately 11–12 months. At birth, the pup is fully formed and immediately capable of swimming and electroreception-based foraging. Sexual maturity is reached at approximately 4–5 years. Low reproductive output — one pup per year per female — means populations recover slowly from depletion. The pup's early independence is an energy advantage for the mother but provides no post-birth parental guidance.

**Constraint**
Cownose rays are constrained by thermal tolerance — they cannot persist in water below approximately 12–15 degrees Celsius and move seasonally to maintain thermal range. Their flat body plan and pectoral-fin swimming locomotion is adapted for open-water cruising rather than precise maneuvering in confined spaces. Prey access depends on suitable sediment substrate — hard rock bottoms contain no accessible buried shellfish. The hard tooth plates, while effective on bivalves, are not suited for all food types. Their high mobility and schooling behavior provide protection from predation but also concentrate the population at predictable locations during migration.

**Cross-Domain Transfer**
The ampullae of Lorenzini represent a detection system that operates on a physical signal type — electric fields — entirely outside the human sensory range. This system reveals a class of environmental information that exists in all aquatic and some terrestrial environments but is invisible to any organism without the appropriate detector. In engineering, electromagnetic field detection underlies technologies from power grid monitoring to medical imaging (EEG, ECG). The biological principle — that weak field gradients carry location and activity information about their source — is the same mechanism exploited in non-invasive bioelectric measurement. The ray solved the problem of locating buried prey with no visual or olfactory cues by evolving a detector sensitive to the prey's own internal electrical output.

**Example Answer 1**

**Question:** How does it find food it can't see?

**Answer:**
Cownose rays find buried clams and oysters using electroreception — small sense organs called the ampullae of Lorenzini clustered on the snout that detect weak electrical fields. Every living animal produces a small electrical field from the activity of its muscles and nerves. Clams buried in sand are invisible and nearly odorless, but they produce a bioelectric signature that the ray's sensors can detect from a short distance. The ray essentially reads the electrical output of a buried animal as a location signal.

**Example Answer 2**

**Question:** Does it sting?

**Answer:**
Cownose rays have a spine at the base of the tail that contains venom and can cause a painful wound if the ray is stepped on or restrained. The spine is a defensive structure rather than an offensive one — the ray does not actively use it to attack. In touch pool settings, cownose rays in managed facilities typically have their spines trimmed to reduce this risk, a process that must be repeated as spines regrow. Observing from outside the pool or following facility guidelines is the appropriate approach.

**Think About This:**
When an animal has a defensive structure, what does its presence tell you about the threats that animal faces in its natural environment?

---

**Example Answer 3**

**Question:** Why does it swim in a school?

**Answer:**
Schools of cownose rays — sometimes numbering in the thousands — provide collective benefits. Each individual's sensory system covers a limited area; the school's combined detection coverage is far larger than any individual's. A predator targeting an individual in a school faces a confusion effect — many similar-sized, similarly moving animals make it difficult to select and track one target. During migration, following the school also provides navigational information. The school is a group structure that produces individual survival benefits without requiring coordinated decision-making.

**Think About This:**
A school has no leader and no plan, yet it moves as a coordinated unit. What does that tell you about how order can emerge from a system without a central controller?
