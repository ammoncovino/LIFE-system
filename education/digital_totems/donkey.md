DONKEY
*Equus asinus*

Order: Perissodactyla
Native to: Northeast Africa
Diet: Herbivore (grasses, browse, hay)
Respiration: Lungs

1. What does it see?
Donkeys have large eyes set wide on the sides of the head, giving a visual field close to 350 degrees with only a narrow blind spot directly behind and immediately in front of the nose. This near-panoramic field is an adaptation to open desert terrain where predators may approach from any direction; the broad binocular overlap is narrower than in forward-facing predators, trading depth perception for coverage.

2. What does it sense?
The donkey's large, long ears can rotate independently through a wide arc, pinpointing the direction of sounds with high accuracy and detecting sounds at greater distances than horses. Tactile sensitivity in the muzzle and lips allows precise selection of individual plant stems, enabling donkeys to forage on sparse, patchy vegetation by picking through substrate at fine resolution.

3. How does it move?
Donkeys are single-toed ungulates moving on a single enlarged digit (digit III) encased in a hard hoof; the hoof wall and frog (the V-shaped pad on the underside) together absorb impact and provide grip. Donkeys are more sure-footed on rocky terrain than horses due to a narrower, more upright hoof shape and a tendency to place each foot with deliberate evaluation before committing full weight — a trait tied to cautious behavioral responses rather than impulsivity.

4. How does it breathe?
Lungs are large relative to body mass; donkeys originated in arid desert ecosystems where thermoregulation through evaporative sweating is critical, and they sweat more efficiently than camels but less copiously than horses. Respiratory rate increases during exertion and during heat loading; the large nasal passages allow high-volume airflow during sustained work.

5. What fuels it?
The donkey's hindgut fermentation system (cecum and large colon) is highly efficient at extracting nutrients from coarse, low-quality forage that horses and ruminants underutilize; this allows donkeys to maintain body condition on sparse desert browse and dry grasses. A handful of low-sugar grass hay provides the fibrous substrate needed to keep hindgut microbial fermentation active; access to mulberry leaves and local browse adds variety consistent with the broad diet of wild ancestors.

6. What limits it?
Donkeys are prone to hyperlipaemia — a dangerous mobilization of fat into the bloodstream — when feed is suddenly restricted or withheld; this makes fasting or abrupt diet changes a metabolic risk rather than a neutral management strategy. Hoof overgrowth in captive settings without abrasive terrain contact requires regular trimming to prevent structural deformity and lameness.

7. What decision is constant?
When does an unfamiliar object or sound warrant standing still and assessing (a common donkey response) rather than fleeing, and what sensory threshold determines which response is triggered? When does the distance to the next forage patch on sparse ground justify the energy expenditure of travel versus continued extraction from the current site?

8. What role does it play?
In their native northeast African range, wild asses browse on drought-resistant shrubs and grasses in rocky desert terrain, preventing any single plant species from monopolizing patchy water-adjacent areas. As prey for lions and leopards in historical ranges, they contributed to the energy transfer between primary producers and apex carnivores in arid-land food webs.
