## Screen 2 — Quick Learn

**What can it see, hear, or feel?**
Horses have the largest eyes of any land mammal relative to skull size. Their eyes sit on the sides of the head, giving a near-360-degree visual field. They see well in dim light due to a reflective layer behind the retina. Two blind spots exist: directly behind the hindquarters and within about 1 meter directly in front of the muzzle. Their large ears rotate up to 180 degrees independently, each tracking separate sound sources at the same time. Whiskers around the muzzle and eyes detect air movement and provide touch information in the blind spot close to the face.

**Where does it live — and why?**
Horses evolved on open grasslands of Central Asia. Open terrain allows long-distance visual detection of predators. Their near-360-degree vision and fast synchronized flight response within a herd are direct adaptations to open grassland predation. They are bulk grazers that crop vegetation to a consistent height, promoting grass tillering and preventing the accumulation of dead material.

**How does it send messages?**
Horses communicate through vocalizations — whinnies, nickers, squeals — and through ear position, tail carriage, and body posture. Each horse has an individual voice recognized by herd members. Ear position is a continuous, readable signal of alertness and social state. Alarm signals from one herd member trigger collective alertness or flight in the whole herd.

**What does it do when something changes?**
When one herd member's alarm signal is detected, others attend to its orientation before the threat is within their own detection range. The herd may orient together, move away slowly, or flee at high speed. At the gallop, each stride compresses and expands the thorax, mechanically driving a breath — reducing the added muscle cost of breathing at speed.

**How does it learn?**
Horses learn routes, food sources, and responses to threats through experience and by following the herd. Young horses learn from adults. The herd's collective knowledge — where water is, which areas are safe, seasonal routes — is the primary information source for new members. Associative learning is strong: horses remember negative experiences at specific locations for years.

**How does it have babies?**
Gestation lasts about 11 months. Usually one foal is born. Foals can stand and walk within hours. They stay close to the mare and herd. Foals are weaned at about 6 months but remain in the herd, continuing to learn from adult herd members.

**What are its limits?**
Dental surfaces wear at a predictable rate over a lifetime. Horses with severely worn molars in old age lose the ability to process fibrous forage efficiently. Colic — disruption of gut motility — is a primary cause of illness and death in horses. It is triggered by diet changes, dehydration, sand ingestion, or prolonged lack of movement.

**What does this teach us?**
The horse's dependence on continuous forage intake — rather than periodic large meals — reveals the design constraints of a fermentation-based digestive system. The system is designed for near-continuous low-volume input, not periodic large meals.

## Screen 3 — Go Deeper

**Perception**
Horses have the largest eyes of any terrestrial mammal relative to skull size, positioned laterally to produce a near-360-degree visual field with binocular vision in a frontal zone of approximately 65 degrees used for judging distances during locomotion. They can see in dim light effectively due to a reflective tapetum lucidum behind the retina but have two blind spots: directly behind the hindquarters and within approximately 1 meter directly in front of the muzzle. The large mobile ear pinnae can rotate up to 180 degrees independently, with each ear tracking separate sound sources simultaneously. Whiskers around the muzzle and eyes are sensory organs that detect air movement and provide touch information in the visual blind spot close to the face. Removing whiskers in groomed horses eliminates this sensory channel entirely.

**Environment**
Horses evolved on the open grasslands of Central Asia. Their range on these grasslands required long-distance visual detection of predators from any direction. Near-360-degree vision and fast synchronized herd flight are direct adaptations to this open-terrain predation pressure. In grassland ecosystems, horses are bulk grazers that crop vegetation to a consistent height, promoting grass tillering and preventing the accumulation of dead material that suppresses new growth. Their hindgut fermentation produces dung with intact, viable seeds, making them long-distance seed dispersers. Dung piles also serve as nutrient-deposition points that support distinct plant and invertebrate communities.

**Signal**
Horses communicate through vocalizations — whinnies, nickers, and squeals — and through ear position, tail carriage, and body posture. Each horse has an individual voice recognized by herd members. Ear position is a continuous, readable signal of alertness, attention, and social state. Alarm signals from one herd member trigger collective alertness or flight. When one horse alerts, others attend to its orientation and reaction before the threat is within their own detection range. This is a distributed threat-response system — collective awareness through individual signal propagation.

**Response**
When one herd member's alarm signal is detected, others attend to its orientation before the threat is within their own detection range. The herd may orient together, move away slowly, or flee at high speed depending on the perceived level of threat. At the gallop, horses use a locomotion-ventilation coupling mechanism where each stride compresses and expands the thorax, mechanically driving a breath cycle that reduces the additional muscular cost of breathing at speed. This coupling makes sustained high-speed locomotion more efficient.

**Learning**
Horses learn routes, food sources, and responses to threats through experience and by following the herd. Young horses learn from adult herd members. The herd's collective knowledge — where water is, which areas are safe, seasonal routes — is the primary information source for new members. Associative learning is strong: horses remember negative experiences at specific locations for years. This long memory for negative associations is an important feature of horse behavior management in captive settings.

**Reproduction**
Gestation lasts about 11 months. Usually one foal is born. Foals are precocial — they can stand and walk within hours of birth. They stay close to the mare and herd. Foals are weaned at about 6 months but remain in the herd, continuing to learn from adult herd members. Sexual maturity occurs at about 1.5 to 2 years, though most horses in managed settings are not bred until 3 to 4 years old to allow physical maturation.

**Constraint**
The anatomy that enables near-continuous grazing — large, hypsodont molars that grind laterally — also creates a constraint: dental surfaces wear at a predictable rate over a lifetime. Horses with severely worn molars in old age lose the ability to process fibrous forage efficiently, leading to body condition decline. Colic — disruption of gut motility — is a primary cause of illness and death in horses, triggered by diet changes, dehydration, sand ingestion, or prolonged lack of movement. The digestive system is designed for near-continuous low-volume input. Disruption of this pattern in either direction creates serious risk.

**Cross-Domain Transfer**
The horse's hindgut — particularly the cecum and ventral colon — houses microbial populations that ferment fibrous plant cell walls into volatile fatty acids, the primary energy currency for the animal. The system is designed for near-continuous low-volume intake rather than periodic large meals. This is a fermentation-based processing system: it requires a steady flow of input to maintain the microbial community that does the actual work. Disrupting that steady flow — by fasting, sudden diet change, or irregular feeding — risks destabilizing the entire system. This principle — that fermentation and process-based systems require steady input rather than batched input — applies in biology, food production, wastewater treatment, and organizational workflow design.

**Example Answer 1**

**Question:** How fast can it run?

**Answer:**
Horses can reach speeds of 70 to 75 km/h at a full gallop for short distances. For sustained running, speeds of 55 to 60 km/h are typical for fit horses. At the gallop, each stride compresses and expands the chest, mechanically driving a breath — this coupling reduces the extra muscular cost of breathing at speed and makes sustained galloping more efficient. Thoroughbred racehorses are selectively bred for this performance. Wild and working horse breeds have lower top speeds but greater endurance.

**Example Answer 2**

**Question:** Why do horses sleep standing up?

**Answer:**
Horses can enter light sleep while standing using a passive stay apparatus — a system of tendons and ligaments in the legs that locks joints in place so the muscles can relax without the horse falling. This allows light sleep without the vulnerability of lying down. Deep sleep — REM sleep — does require lying down, and horses do lie down for short periods. However, rising from a lying position takes time and is difficult on soft ground. For an animal that needs to flee quickly in open terrain, minimizing time spent lying down reduces risk.

**Example Answer 3**

**Question:** What is colic?

**Answer:**
Colic refers to abdominal pain in horses, most commonly caused by disruption of gut motility — the muscular contractions that move food through the digestive tract. Horses have a long, non-reversible digestive tract with several anatomical bottlenecks where gas or material can become trapped. Causes include diet changes, dehydration, sand ingestion from grazing on sandy soil, or prolonged lack of movement. The hindgut fermentation system is designed for near-continuous low-volume input. Disrupting that steady flow creates risk. Colic is the primary cause of illness-related death in horses.
