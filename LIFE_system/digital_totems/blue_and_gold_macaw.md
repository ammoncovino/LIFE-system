## Screen 2 — Quick Learn

**What can it see, hear, or feel?**
Blue-and-gold macaws see colors that humans cannot see. Their eyes detect ultraviolet light. Some feather patterns are invisible to us but visible to them. They have sharp eyesight and can track moving objects far away. Their hearing is strong. Their beaks are sensitive to touch.

**Where does it live — and why?**
It lives in forests, woodland edges, and areas near rivers. Its range covers South America and into Central America. It needs large trees for nesting and sleeping. Open areas near forests give it room to fly and find food.

**How does it send messages?**
It uses loud calls to stay in touch with flock members over long distances. Each macaw has its own voice. Flock members can tell each other apart by sound alone. Pairs use special calls just to stay close to each other.

**What does it do when something changes?**
It calls loudly, then takes off with the flock. Macaws are quick to react. When something startles them, the flock flies up and circles while everyone looks for danger. The whole group pays attention at once. That makes it very hard to sneak up on them.

**How does it learn?**
Macaws are very smart birds. Young macaws watch adults crack open nuts and seeds. They learn by watching. They also learn calls from other macaws. Calls are not fully built-in — they are partly learned. The flock and the pair are the main teachers.

**How does it have babies?**
Pairs stay together for life. They raise one to three chicks each year inside a tree-hole nest. Both parents feed and protect the chicks. The chicks stay in the nest for several months before they learn to fly.

**What are its limits?**
It needs large forests with tree holes for nesting. It cannot survive without a flock and a stable pair bond. Loud noise and disturbance near the nest can stop breeding from succeeding.

**What does this teach us?**
A bird's senses — seeing ultraviolet light, hearing specific calls — build a completely different picture of the same world. What looks real depends on the sensors you have.

**Think About This**
If your eyes could see ultraviolet light, what might you notice about birds, flowers, or sunscreen that is invisible to you right now?

## Screen 3 — Go Deeper

**Perception**
Blue-and-gold macaws have four types of color-sensing cells in their eyes. Humans only have three. This lets macaws see ultraviolet light. Some feather patterns and flower markings look plain to us but carry extra information for macaws. Their eyes sit on the sides of their head. This gives them a wide view to spot danger. Their eyes also work well up close when they use their beak to handle food. The beak tip and tongue are packed with touch sensors. These give the bird very precise feel when cracking hard seeds. Their hearing is sharp, especially for the sounds their flock makes.

**Environment**
Blue-and-gold macaws live in lowland tropical forests, forest edges, and river-side forests. Their range covers eastern Panama, Venezuela, Colombia, Brazil, Ecuador, Peru, Bolivia, and Trinidad. They need large trees with existing holes for nesting — they cannot dig their own. They fly long distances between food patches, clay banks, and roosting trees. They can live in landscapes that mix forest and open land. But they cannot survive in areas with no trees left for nesting.

**Signal**
Macaw calls are complex and personally unique. A pair-contact call lets mates find each other in a noisy flock. Flock calls move the group together. An alarm call sends everyone flying. Research shows individual macaws have distinct voices and change their calls in different situations. Captive macaws often copy human speech or sounds from their environment. But copying sounds seems different from the bird's own natural communication. The copied sounds do not carry meaning for the bird the way its own calls do.

**Response**
When one bird spots danger, others watch how that bird reacts. They respond before the threat is even within their own view. This spreads a warning through the whole flock fast. The flock acts like a shared alarm system. At clay banks — places where macaws eat mineral-rich clay — the flock may land, take off, land again, and repeat several times before actually feeding. They are checking for danger the whole time.

**Learning**
Macaw brains are large for their body size. This supports strong learning and problem-solving. Young birds learn which seeds to crack and which plants to avoid by watching adults. Vocal learning is also established: young macaws develop their calls through interaction with adults. This is similar in some ways to how children learn language. Macaws live 50 or more years. Over a long life they build up a huge store of knowledge about their environment.

**Reproduction**
Blue-and-gold macaws form pair bonds that last across many breeding seasons and likely for life. They defend and reuse the same tree-hole nest year after year. A typical clutch holds two to three eggs. Incubation takes about 26 days. Chicks hatch helpless and need a lot of parental feeding. They can fly at about 97 to 100 days old. Even after leaving the nest, young macaws stay loosely connected to their parents inside the flock. First breeding usually happens at 3 to 4 years old.

**Constraint**
Blue-and-gold macaws cannot breed without large tree holes. Those holes are rare and other species compete for them. Their large body needs a lot of food. Foraging well depends on being part of a flock — the group finds food and spots predators together. Losing a mate is a serious problem because the pair bond is how they coordinate raising young. Their slow reproductive rate — often one successful chick per year or less — means a population takes a long time to recover after losses.

**Cross-Domain Transfer**
The macaw's four-color vision shows that what sensors you have decides what is real to you. The same environment holds different information depending on who is looking. This idea goes beyond biology. Tools built with different sensors — radio telescopes, infrared cameras, spectrometers — reveal features of the same place that other tools miss. Think of it like a 5th grader would: adding more types of detectors always uncovers structure that was invisible before. What looks empty or plain at one sensor range often has rich hidden detail at another.

**Example Answer 1**

**Question:** Can it actually talk?

**Answer:**
Blue-and-gold macaws can copy human speech quite accurately. They use a vocal organ called the syrinx to control sound. In captivity, macaws often learn words and phrases. Whether the sounds carry meaning for the bird the way they do for humans is not known. The evidence suggests copying sounds is different from the bird's natural communication. The bird is producing learned sounds. What the bird is processing when it makes those sounds is a separate question.

**Think About This:**
What is the difference between producing a sound correctly and understanding what that sound means?

---

**Example Answer 2**

**Question:** Why does it eat clay?

**Answer:**
Macaws visit exposed riverbank clay deposits and eat the clay. The best explanation is that clay particles stick to toxins in some seeds and fruit. Those toxins are part of how plants defend themselves. Eating clay may let macaws eat foods that would otherwise be too toxic. Clay banks are also social gathering spots where many species come together. That creates a group-detection benefit — more eyes watching for danger.

**Think About This:**
If a food is toxic but a macaw found a way to cancel out that toxin, what does that tell you about the ongoing back-and-forth between animals and the plants they eat?

---

**Example Answer 3**

**Question:** How long do they live?

**Answer:**
Blue-and-gold macaws can live 30 to 60 or more years in captivity. Wild lifespans are typically shorter because of predators and environmental challenges. Their long lifespan goes hand-in-hand with slow reproduction — they raise very few offspring per year. They also spend a long time caring for each chick. Long-lived animals often hold and pass on a large store of knowledge built up over many years.

**Think About This:**
How does a long lifespan change the role of one animal in a group? What does an older animal know that a younger one does not?
