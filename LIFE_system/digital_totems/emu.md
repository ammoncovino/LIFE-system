## Screen 2 — Quick Learn

**What can it see, hear, or feel?**
Emus have large eyes positioned on the sides of the head. This gives a wide monocular field on each side and a usable forward binocular zone for close inspection of food. Vision is sharp in bright daylight. Emus can detect slow movement at long distances across flat open terrain. They have an inflatable neck sac that produces low-frequency booming calls used in long-distance communication. The vibrational component of these calls may travel through the ground and be detected by other emus at range.

**Where does it live — and why?**
Emus are native to Australia. They live across a wide range of habitats — open grasslands, scrubland, and open woodland. Their bodies are built for covering large distances on foot. Long legs with large hip and thigh muscles power a striding gait. Legs carry no feathers below the thigh, which reduces heat buildup during sustained walking and trotting.

**How does it send messages?**
Emus use low-frequency booming calls that carry over long distances. These calls are produced by an inflatable neck sac. The vibrations may also transmit through the ground. During breeding, females make loud drumming sounds. Visual signals include body posture and the direction of the head and neck.

**What does it do when something changes?**
When an unusual sound or shape appears, emus may approach and investigate or immediately withdraw depending on the stimulus. They can sustain high speeds when fleeing. Their large inner toe carries a powerful claw that can be used defensively.

**How does it learn?**
Emus are opportunistic feeders that shift their diet with the seasons — seeds and fruit when available, insects during breeding and chick-rearing periods. This dietary flexibility requires learning which food sources are currently available and where. Young emus stay with their father for up to 18 months, learning routes and food sources by following him.

**How does it have babies?**
The male incubates the eggs and raises the chicks. Females lay 5 to 15 eggs in a ground nest. The male incubates for about 8 weeks without eating, surviving on stored fat. Chicks hatch with striped plumage and stay with the father for up to 18 months.

**What are its limits?**
Emus cannot fly. Once contained, escape is impossible by air. Their ability to cover large distances on foot is their primary mobility strategy in the wild. They require grit or small stones to help grind hard seeds in their digestive system. Without access to grit in captive settings, digestion is impaired.

**What does this teach us?**
The emu's inability to fly, combined with its capacity to run dozens of kilometers in a day, shows how energy is allocated differently across bird species. Not flying frees up body resources for sustained ground locomotion and long-distance seed dispersal.

## Screen 3 — Go Deeper

**Perception**
Emus have large eyes positioned on the sides of the head, providing a wide monocular field on each side and a functional forward binocular zone used when approaching and inspecting food items at close range. Vision is sharp in bright daylight. Emus can detect slow movement at long distances across flat open terrain. Emus possess an inflatable neck sac that produces low-frequency booming calls used in long-distance communication. The vibrational component of these calls may transmit through the ground and be detected by other emus at range. Their sense of smell is functional but not dominant. Food detection relies primarily on vision, with close inspection of items before ingestion.

**Environment**
Emus are native to Australia and live across a wide range of habitats including open grasslands, scrubland, and open woodland. They are long-distance nomads: wild emu movements track rainfall and resource pulses, redistributing plant propagules across the Australian landscape. Seeds consumed whole pass intact through the gut and are deposited far from the parent plant in dung that also fertilizes the germination site. Their movements across hundreds of kilometers make them important seed dispersers at a landscape scale. Emus are ratites — birds in an ancient lineage that includes ostriches and rheas — all of which are large, flightless, and built for ground locomotion.

**Signal**
Emus use low-frequency booming calls that carry over long distances. These calls are produced by an inflatable neck sac. The vibrational component may also travel through the ground. During breeding, females produce loud drumming sounds. Visual signals include body posture and the direction of the head and neck. The emu's large size and upright posture make body orientation a readable social signal at distance across open terrain.

**Response**
Emus use an avian flow-through respiratory system, with air sacs serving as bellows. This pushes fresh air continuously in one direction over the gas-exchange surface. During heat, emus use panting — rapid shallow breathing — that increases evaporative cooling from moist respiratory surfaces. When an unusual sound or shape appears, emus may approach and investigate or immediately withdraw depending on the stimulus. They can sustain high speeds when fleeing. The large inner toe carries a powerful claw that can be used defensively.

**Learning**
Emus are opportunistic feeders that shift diet composition seasonally. Seeds and fruit dominate when available. Insects — particularly caterpillars and grasshoppers — supply concentrated protein during breeding and chick-rearing periods. This dietary flexibility requires ongoing learning about which food sources are currently productive and where. Young emus stay with their father for up to 18 months, learning routes and food sources by following him. This extended parental association is the primary context for learning in this species.

**Reproduction**
The male incubates the eggs and raises the chicks — an unusual arrangement. Females lay 5 to 15 eggs in a ground nest. The male incubates for about 8 weeks, during which he does not eat and survives on stored fat. He loses up to one-third of his body weight during incubation. Chicks hatch with striped plumage that provides camouflage. They stay with the father for up to 18 months. The striped pattern fades as chicks grow. Females may mate with multiple males in a season.

**Constraint**
Emus cannot fly, making escape from enclosed areas impossible once contained. This behavioral inflexibility is managed in the wild by the long-distance nomadism that characterizes wild population movement. The proventricular portion of the digestive system is adapted for grit-assisted mechanical breakdown of hard seeds. Lack of access to grit or stones impairs digestion in captive settings. These are recurring decisions that shape daily movement.

Emus are long-distance seed dispersers. Seeds consumed whole pass intact through the gut and are deposited far from the parent plant in dung that fertilizes the germination site. Their movements across hundreds of kilometers track rainfall and resource pulses, redistributing plant propagules across the Australian landscape. **Example Answer 1**

**Question:** Why can't it fly?

**Answer:**
Emus are ratites — an ancient lineage of birds that evolved large bodies and strong legs for ground locomotion rather than flight. Flying requires large pectoral muscles and lightweight bones. Emus have reduced wings and dense bones suited for running, not lifting off. The energy that would go into flight capability goes instead into sustained running at 48 to 50 km/h and long-distance travel across the Australian landscape. Not flying is not a failure — it is a different allocation of body resources.

**Example Answer 2**

**Question:** What does the male do after the eggs are laid?

**Answer:**
After the female lays 5 to 15 eggs in a ground nest, the male takes over completely. He incubates the eggs for about 8 weeks, during which he does not eat. He survives on stored fat accumulated before incubation begins. He loses up to one-third of his body weight during this period. He also cares for the chicks after hatching and keeps them with him for up to 18 months. The female plays no role in incubation or chick-rearing — an unusual arrangement among birds.

**Think About This:**

**Example Answer 3**

**Question:** How does it help plants grow?

**Answer:**
Emus eat seeds whole. Many of those seeds pass intact through the gut and are deposited — in dung that also provides nutrients — far from the parent plant. This is seed dispersal: the emu's movement across hundreds of kilometers transports seeds to new locations where they can germinate without competing with the parent plant. Emu dung acts as a fertilizer patch at the deposition site. The emu's nomadic movement across Australia tracks rainfall and resource pulses, redistributing plant propagules at a landscape scale.
