## Screen 2 — Quick Learn

**What can it see, hear, or feel?**
Giraffes have large eyes set far apart on the sides of their head. This gives them a wide view without turning. They can see color and spot movement from far away. Their hearing is good. They make low-frequency sounds that are sometimes too low for humans to hear clearly.

**Where does it live — and why?**
Giraffes live in African savannas, open woodlands, and grasslands. These open areas let them see far across the land. That matters for spotting predators. They depend on trees — especially Acacia trees — for food.

**How does it send messages?**
Giraffes are mostly quiet. They communicate through body posture, closeness, and low sounds including hums and rumbles. Males test each other through a behavior called necking — swinging their necks and heads against each other.

**What does it do when something changes?**
It watches. Giraffes often detect threats from far away and stand still, looking, before deciding to move. Other animals on the savanna watch giraffes. When a giraffe looks alert or starts running, other species nearby take notice and respond.

**How does it learn?**
Calves follow their mothers and learn movement patterns, food sources, and how to react to threats through watching and experience. Social knowledge — such as which areas are safe or which plants to eat — builds up over years.

**How does it have babies?**
Females give birth to one calf at a time, standing up. The calf can stand and walk within hours of birth. Calves stay with their mother and are also left in loose nursery groups while mothers go to feed.

**What are its limits?**
A giraffe's height creates mechanical problems. Drinking requires spreading or bending the front legs to reach water — a vulnerable position that exposes it to predators. It cannot enter dense forest. Its large size requires a very large amount of food each day.

**What does this teach us?**
Every adaptation has a cost. The same body feature that gives an advantage in one situation creates a problem in another. Design is always a set of tradeoffs.

**Think About This**
The giraffe's long neck helps it eat from tall trees, but it makes drinking from a pool dangerous. Can you think of a design in your own life where the thing that helps you also makes something else harder?

## Screen 3 — Go Deeper

**Perception**
Giraffe eyes are large and sit on the sides of the head. This gives nearly 360-degree vision with very few blind spots. This setup is built for spotting predators in open habitats, not for precise depth perception. Their eyesight is sharp for detecting things at long distance. The giraffe's height — eyes at over 5 meters up — extends how far they can see beyond what ground-level animals can access. This height makes giraffes useful as lookouts. Other savanna animals including zebras and wildebeest have been seen checking giraffe alertness behavior as a signal of nearby danger. Low-frequency sounds below the human hearing range may let giraffes communicate across long distances.

**Environment**
Giraffes are built for open-canopy savanna and dry woodland where tall trees — especially Acacia and Commiphora species — are available. Their range follows the distribution of tall browsable plants. They are not migratory in a fixed-route sense. Seasonal movements follow food availability. Open habitat is a sensory need as much as a food need — dense forest blocks the long-distance vision that is central to how they avoid predators. Water access is needed but not daily. Giraffes can go several days without drinking by getting moisture from browse.

**Signal**
Giraffe social signals rely heavily on posture, closeness, and physical contact. Necking — males swinging their necks and heads against each other with significant force — is a dominance contest. It establishes which male gets mating access. Calves and mothers stay in contact through closeness and nuzzling. Vocalizations are infrequent compared to many large mammals. The body itself — its direction, movement, and height — is the main signal. When a giraffe lifts its gaze and focuses it, nearby animals of many species read this posture as a threat indicator.

**Response**
Giraffe responses to threat are organized by distance. Far away: they orient and watch. At middle distance: they may begin moving away slowly. Up close: they run — at up to 55 to 60 km/h. Giraffes can also deliver powerful defensive kicks that have been documented killing lions. The drinking posture — front legs spread or one leg bent forward — is very vulnerable. The head is near the ground, the field of view is reduced, and fast escape is difficult. Giraffes scan intensively and may spend many minutes approaching a water source before committing to drink.

**Learning**
Calves stay close to their mothers for 14 to 18 months. They learn movement routes, food choices, and social behavior by watching. Spatial knowledge of water sources and food patches within a home range develops over years. Long-lived individuals carry a large store of knowledge about seasonal resources, predator patterns, and safe travel routes. Social groups do not have a rigid structure, but individuals recognize familiar animals and prefer them.

**Reproduction**
Pregnancy lasts about 15 months — one of the longest of any land mammal. Single calves are born weighing about 100 kg and standing nearly 2 meters tall. Calves can run within hours of birth. This is critical because newborns face heavy predation pressure. Females form loose nursery groups — one or a few calves watched by one adult while the others feed. This cooperative setup reduces the cost of staying vigilant per adult. Sexual maturity comes at 4 to 5 years, though males typically do not compete effectively for mating until they are older.

**Constraint**
The giraffe's body creates hard limits. The heart must generate unusually high blood pressure — about twice that of most large mammals — to push blood up the long neck to the brain. At the same time, the system must prevent too much pressure when the head drops down to ground level. A network of small blood vessels called the rete mirabile handles this. The long neck also requires very long nerve pathways. The recurrent laryngeal nerve, for example, travels from the brain down the neck to the chest and back up to the voice box — a path of several meters. These are structural limits that cannot be changed without rebuilding the whole system.

**Cross-Domain Transfer**
The giraffe's cardiovascular system shows how one structural feature creates engineering requirements throughout the whole body. A long neck needs a stronger heart, a pressure-regulation system at the base of the brain, and specialized blood vessels in the legs to prevent blood pooling. This principle — that one design change sends new requirements through the whole system — shows up in engineering, software architecture, and organizational design. Think of it like a 5th grader would: change one part in a tightly connected system and everything else has to adjust.

**Example Answer 1**

**Question:** Why is its neck so long?

**Answer:**
The long neck lets the giraffe reach leaves at heights other browsers cannot reach — especially the upper branches of tall Acacia trees. It also raises the eyes high above the ground, extending how far the giraffe can see across open savanna. Research also shows the neck is used in dominance contests between males, called necking. The neck is one structure doing several jobs at once. But it also creates serious engineering problems — especially for the heart and blood vessels.

**Think About This:**
When one body part does several jobs, what happens to the whole system if that part is changed or damaged?

---

**Example Answer 2**

**Question:** How does its heart work?

**Answer:**
A giraffe's heart weighs about 11 kilograms. It generates blood pressure roughly twice that of most large mammals. This pressure is needed to push blood up the length of the neck to the brain. At the same time, special pressure-sensing valves and blood vessel networks prevent the brain from being flooded with high pressure when the head drops down to ground level. The whole system is a solution to the mechanical problem created by the neck's length.

**Think About This:**
What does it mean for an entire system — heart, blood vessels, brain — to be reshaped by one structural feature like neck length?

---

**Example Answer 3**

**Question:** Does it sleep?

**Answer:**
Giraffes sleep but in short episodes. They typically total only 30 minutes to 2 hours of sleep per day, spread across several brief periods. They often sleep standing, though they can lie down. Lying down takes time and makes getting up quickly difficult — a real danger when predators are present. How much a giraffe sleeps and what position it sleeps in appears to be shaped by predation risk, not a fixed biological need.

**Think About This:**
How does an animal's environment shape something as basic as how and how much it sleeps?
