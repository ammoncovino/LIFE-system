## Screen 2 — Quick Learn

**What can it see, hear, or feel?**
Alpacas have eyes on the sides of their head. Their pupils are horizontal and elongated. This gives them a wide view — over 300 degrees — useful for spotting predators across open mountain plateaus. Their upper lip is highly mobile and can feel and sort food by texture before eating. Their ears rotate independently to locate sounds. Ear position also signals social status and alertness to the herd.

**Where does it live — and why?**
Wild alpacas and their ancestor the vicuña live on the Andes plateaus of South America, between 3,500 and 5,000 meters elevation. The air is thin and cold there. Alpacas have blood cells that extract oxygen very efficiently from thin air. Their dense fiber coat handles the cold. In captivity at lower elevations, this same physiology makes them very efficient during exertion.

**How does it send messages?**
Alpacas use ear position to signal alertness and social rank. Humming is a common vocalization used in many contexts. Alarm calls warn the herd of danger. Spitting is used in competition for resources or to set limits on social interactions.

**What does it do when something changes?**
When something alarming appears, alpacas call out and the herd moves together. They are vigilant on open terrain where threats can come from far away. The group's collective alertness gives all members an advantage.

**How does it learn?**
Young alpacas learn by following adults in the herd. They observe foraging routes and social behaviors. Herd membership is the primary context for learning what to eat, where to go, and how to respond to other animals.

**How does it have babies?**
Alpacas typically have one cria — a baby alpaca — per year. Gestation lasts about 11 to 12 months. Crias are born able to stand and walk within hours. They stay close to their mothers and herd.

**What are its limits?**
Alpacas are at risk of heat stress at lower altitudes. Their dense fiber coat — adapted for Andean cold — retains heat in warm humid environments without shade and water access. Dental misalignment is a recurring issue in captive settings. The incisors grow continuously and need managed trimming.

**What does this teach us?**
The alpaca's blood chemistry shows how the body adapts to extreme environments over generations. Features built for one environment — thin air, extreme cold — create both advantages and limits when the environment changes.

## Screen 3 — Go Deeper

**Perception**
Alpacas have laterally placed eyes with a wide visual field exceeding 300 degrees. This is adapted to detecting movement across open Andean plateaus where predators approach from distance. The pupils are elongated horizontally, expanding peripheral depth of field while the animal grazes with its head lowered. The upper lip is highly mobile and is used to sort forage by texture before swallowing. Ear movement is pronounced and independent. Rotating ears allow localization of sound sources. Ear position communicates social status and alertness state within the herd.

**Environment**
Alpacas evolved at altitudes of 3,500 to 5,000 meters in the Andes Mountains of South America. The high Andean plateau is open, cold, and has thin air. Predators can approach from any direction across wide visual horizons. This environment selected for wide visual coverage and efficient oxygen extraction. At lower elevations in captive settings, alpacas retain their high-altitude physiology — including highly efficient red blood cells and respiratory recovery after movement. The wide open terrain they evolved in is why their eyes and alertness behavior are tuned the way they are.

**Signal**
Alpacas use a range of vocalizations. Humming is the most common and appears in many social contexts. Alarm calls warn the herd of danger. Spitting is used in competition over resources and to establish social limits. Ear position is a continuous, readable signal of social state and alertness. The highly mobile upper lip is also used in social investigation — sniffing and lip-touching are common during close social interactions.

**Response**
When a predator or alarming stimulus is detected, the herd responds together. Alarm calls trigger group movement away. On open Andean terrain, the response to distant threat is visual tracking and group alertness. Up close, the response is rapid group movement. The herd's collective attention covers more ground than any individual's. Alpacas prefer to assess before fleeing when the threat is distant — consistent with open-terrain prey animal strategies where false alarms are costly.

**Learning**
Young alpacas learn by staying near adults in the herd. Foraging routes, social behaviors, and responses to threats are acquired through observation and following experienced herd members. Spatial memory of the home range develops through movement with the herd. Alpacas are modified ruminants and must also learn which plants to eat and which to avoid — this knowledge is primarily socially transmitted.

**Reproduction**
Alpacas typically produce one cria per year. Gestation is approximately 11 to 12 months. Crias are born able to stand and walk within hours — a precocial characteristic. They stay close to their mothers and within the herd. Social stability of the herd affects cria survival. In managed settings, breeding is controlled to optimize fiber production and herd health.

**Constraint**
Dental malocclusion is a recurring constraint. Alpaca incisors grow continuously. Misalignment reduces foraging efficiency over time and requires managed trimming in captive settings. Heat stress is a significant risk at lower altitudes. The dense fiber coat adapted for Andean cold retains heat in warm humid environments. Without shade and water access, alpacas can overheat quickly. These are the costs of being built for a specific high-altitude, cold environment.

**Cross-Domain Transfer**
Alpacas are modified ruminants — camelids have a three-compartment stomach rather than four — and they ferment plant material very efficiently. They extract more nutrition per unit of forage than sheep or goats. This makes them well-suited to sparse, low-quality Andean grasses. In their native grassland ecosystems, alpacas and their wild ancestor the vicuña graze selectively in ways that can maintain grass diversity when stocking density stays within carrying capacity. Their dung is concentrated in communal latrine areas, creating localized nutrient hotspots that differ chemically from surrounding grazed soil. The idea that where and how nutrients are deposited shapes the ecology of the whole area is a general principle in ecosystem management and design.

**Example Answer 1**

**Question:** Why does it spit?

**Answer:**
Alpacas spit as a social signal — not primarily as a defense against people. Spitting is used to set social limits, to compete over resources like food or space, and to express discomfort. The spit comes from the stomach and can be quite strong-smelling. It is a communication tool within the herd. Animals that spit frequently are usually expressing a social signal to another alpaca, not to a human observer.

**Example Answer 2**

**Question:** How does it stay warm at high altitude?

**Answer:**
Alpacas have an extremely dense fiber coat. Each fiber is hollow, trapping air and providing insulation against the cold, thin air of the high Andes at 3,500 to 5,000 meters elevation. Their blood cells also have an unusually high capacity to carry oxygen from thin mountain air. These two features — insulating fiber and efficient blood — are built for the cold, thin-air environment of the Andean plateau.

**Think About This:**
A feature built for one environment creates both advantages and problems in another. What happens to the alpaca's thermal advantage when it moves to a warmer, lower-altitude setting?

---

**Example Answer 3**

**Question:** Why are its feet soft instead of hard?

**Answer:**
Alpacas walk on padded two-toed feet with a leathery sole pad rather than the hard hooves of sheep or goats. This pad causes less soil disturbance on the Andean grassland surface. It is suited for travel over high-altitude uneven terrain. The tradeoff is limited traction on steep wet rock. Alpacas also use a lateral gait — moving both limbs on the same side together — which is efficient for sustained travel over uneven terrain at high altitude.
