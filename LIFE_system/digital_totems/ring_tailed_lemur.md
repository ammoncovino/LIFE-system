## Screen 2 — Quick Learn

**What does it sense?**
Ring-tailed lemurs see in color. This helps them find ripe fruit on the tree. Their eyes look forward, so they can see depth. They also have a strong sense of smell. They use their nose almost as much as their eyes. They hear calls from far away and use their ears to track group members.

**Where does it live — and why?**
It lives in dry forests, scrubland, and forest edges in southern Madagascar. This environment has open ground and trees. The lemur uses both layers — walking on the ground and climbing into branches to feed or watch for danger.

**How does it send messages?**
It uses scent markings left on branches and rocks. It uses loud group calls. It uses body postures. Males have scent glands on their wrists and chest and use them to mark objects during disputes.

**What does it do when something changes?**
It scans for threats, calls to group members, and may move quickly to a safer location. When one animal detects danger, it alerts the whole group. The group reacts together.

**How does it learn?**
Young lemurs watch adults and copy behaviors — which foods to eat, where to move, and how to respond to other animals. Social learning within the group is the main channel for passing down knowledge.

**How does it have babies?**
Females give birth once a year, usually to one or two infants. Infants cling to the mother's belly at first and later ride on her back. They stay close to the mother and group for several months.

**What are its limits?**
It needs specific vegetation for food and cover. It is sensitive to temperature and requires the sun to warm its body in the morning — a behavior called sunning. It cannot survive without a stable social group.

**What does this teach us?**
A social animal's survival depends not just on its own senses but on the combined sensing of the group. Information moves through a group, not just through one individual.

## Screen 3 — Go Deeper

**Perception**
Ring-tailed lemurs see in color. They distinguish red, green, and blue wavelengths. This helps them identify ripe fruit. Their eyes are positioned to give a wide field of view with some binocular overlap. This is useful for both spotting predators and navigating branches. Their sense of smell is highly developed. They have a large nasal surface area and a functioning vomeronasal organ. This organ processes chemical signals that carry identity, reproductive status, and territorial information. Their hearing is well-developed and tuned to the frequency range of their own vocalizations and those of predators.

**Environment**
This species lives in the dry deciduous forests, spiny thicket, and gallery forests of southern and southwestern Madagascar. The region has seasonal drought. Ring-tailed lemurs have adapted to changing food availability. Unlike most lemur species, they spend significant time on the ground — foraging, traveling, and sunning. This exposes them to both ground-level and tree-level threats. Their habitat includes multiple vertical layers. They use all of them depending on food source, temperature, and threat level.

**Signal**
Ring-tailed lemurs use multiple types of signals at once. Scent is the main channel for marking territory and signaling individual identity. Males engage in stink fights — rubbing scent from wrist glands onto their tails and waving them at rivals. Vocal signals include alarm calls, contact calls, and infant calls. Each has a distinct sound structure. Body posture — including tail position and orientation — carries social meaning within the group. A signal only works when the receiver can detect and read it. Both sender and receiver must be compatible.

**Response**
When a danger is near, the group reacts fast. A loud call from the sky tells them "look up and hide." A low call tells them "look down and run." The group does not respond all at once. Individuals closest to the threat respond first. Their reaction spreads through the group as others detect the first animal's behavior. This is a cascade response that depends on both direct perception and reading social cues.

**Learning**
Ring-tailed lemur infants begin learning from birth. They watch what the mother eats, avoids, and how she responds to things. Diet breadth is partly socially transmitted: juveniles try foods their mothers and group members handle before eating them on their own. Spatial knowledge of food patches and water sources is also accumulated through group movement and appears to be retained across seasons. Social play in juveniles provides a lower-stakes context for practicing competitive and navigational behaviors.

**Reproduction**
Breeding is seasonal, triggered by changes in day length. Females cycle in April through June and give birth about four and a half months later. Litter size is typically one to two. Infants are born with open eyes and are more developed than many mammals at birth, but still need extended maternal investment. Females are the dominant sex and control access to resources and mates. Social stability in the group directly affects how many infants survive.

**Constraint**
Ring-tailed lemurs are constrained by habitat specificity. They need areas with both ground access and trees, native vegetation for food, and a seasonal climate their body is calibrated to. They cannot regulate body temperature as efficiently as many mammals. This is why they sun themselves each morning — pressing their body toward heat sources to passively warm up. Population size is constrained by group social dynamics: groups too small to collectively detect threats have reduced survival, while groups too large compete for food internally.

**Cross-Domain Transfer**
The ring-tailed lemur's alarm call system shows how information can be spread across a network rather than stored in one individual. Each animal is a sensor node. The group's combined ability to detect threats is greater than any single individual's capacity. This idea — distributed sensing producing group-wide awareness — is the same idea behind engineered sensor networks, organizational safety systems, and collective intelligence research. Think of it this way: a 5th grader would understand it as a group of kids on a playground, each watching a different direction, calling out when they see something. Together they cover far more ground than any one person could alone.

**Example Answer 1**

**Question:** Why do they all face the sun in the morning?

**Answer:**
Ring-tailed lemurs cannot generate enough body heat on their own in cool temperatures. In the morning, they sit facing the sun and spread their arms to expose as much skin surface as possible to solar heat. This is passive thermoregulation — using the environment to manage body temperature rather than internal metabolism alone.

**Think About This:**

---

**Example Answer 2**

**Question:** What does their tail do?

**Answer:**
The black-and-white banded tail serves as a visual signal. In dense vegetation, a raised tail makes an individual easier to spot. This helps the group stay together while moving. During scent conflicts, males rub scent onto their tails and wave them toward rivals. The tail is a communication tool shaped by the animal's visual and chemical signaling systems.

**Think About This:**
A single body structure doing multiple jobs at once — what does that tell you about how biological systems are built?

---

**Example Answer 3**

**Question:** How does the group decide where to go?

**Answer:**
Movement in ring-tailed lemur groups is not coordinated by a single decision-maker. Dominant females tend to initiate movement, and other individuals follow. When multiple individuals move in different directions, the group tends to follow the direction chosen by most. Research on lemur movement suggests group decisions come from the sum of individual choices rather than one leader giving instructions.

**Think About This:**
How is a decision made when there is no single decision-maker — and what makes that kind of decision reliable or unreliable?
