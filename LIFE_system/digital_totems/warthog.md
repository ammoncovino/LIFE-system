## Screen 2 — Quick Learn

**What can it see, hear, or feel?**
Warthogs have eyes positioned high and laterally on the skull. This gives a wide field of view that allows detection of predators from nearly all directions while grazing with the head down. Color vision is limited compared to primates, but motion detection in peripheral vision is sharp. Large, mobile ears rotate independently to localize sounds — detecting predator approach even in tall grass. A well-developed sense of smell is used to locate underground food sources and to read chemical signals left by other warthogs.

**Where does it live — and why?**
Warthogs live across Sub-Saharan Africa in savannas, grasslands, and open woodlands. They depend on open terrain for long-distance threat detection and on underground food sources — grass rhizomes and roots — accessible by rooting. They shelter in burrows to retain heat overnight and to escape predators.

**How does it send messages?**
Warthogs communicate through grunts, snorts, and vocalizations. When fleeing predators, they hold their tail erect — a visible signal to other warthogs in the group. Chemical signals read through smell communicate individual identity and reproductive state. Body posture signals social rank.

**What does it do when something changes?**
When alarmed, warthogs run fast — up to 55 km/h — with the tail held straight up. This raised tail signals alarm to the group. They head for a burrow when one is available. When cornered, they can defend themselves with their tusks. When does remaining near the foraging patch outweigh the growing risk of predator detection in open ground? This decision shapes daily activity.

**How does it learn?**
Young warthogs learn foraging areas and responses to threats by staying close to adults. The group's collective alertness — more eyes and ears scanning — provides all members with earlier detection of threats. Experience shapes which food patches are most reliable and which routes to water are safest.

**How does it have babies?**
Females typically give birth to two to four piglets. Gestation lasts about 170 days. Piglets are born in a burrow. They emerge after about one week and stay close to the mother. The family group shelters together in burrows overnight.

**What are its limits?**
Body size and limited insulating fat make warthogs vulnerable to cold. They shelter in burrows to retain heat overnight. Dental wear sets a functional limit on lifespan. The large incisors and molars used for root-digging erode over years of contact with abrasive soil. When the teeth wear down, the ability to forage efficiently is compromised.

**What does this teach us?**
The warthog's body reveals the tradeoffs between finding food underground and staying alert above it. The high eye placement, mobile ears, and the ability to run fast all serve the same function — detecting and responding to threats while foraging in open terrain.

## Screen 3 — Go Deeper

**Perception**
Warthogs have eyes positioned high and laterally on the skull, providing a wide field of view that allows detection of predators from nearly all directions while grazing with the head down. Color vision is limited compared to primates, but motion detection in peripheral vision is sharp. Their large, mobile ears rotate independently to localize sounds, detecting approach from predators even in tall grass. A well-developed sense of smell is used to locate underground food sources — grass rhizomes, roots, and bulbs — and to read chemical signals left by other warthogs. The sensory setup is built for an animal that spends significant time with its head down, rooting in soil, while remaining alert to threats from above and around.

**Environment**
Warthogs live in Sub-Saharan Africa in savannas, grasslands, and open woodlands. They depend on open terrain for long-distance threat detection and on underground food sources accessible by rooting. Grasses and grass rhizomes form the bulk of the diet in the wet season. Roots, bulbs, and bark become primary energy sources during dry months when grasses die back. Insects and carrion are consumed opportunistically, contributing protein when plant material is nutritionally lean. Warthogs disturb and aerate soil through continuous rooting, exposing seeds and invertebrates that become available to other species. Their carcasses and dung supply nutrients that cycle through the savanna system.

**Signal**
Warthogs communicate through grunts, snorts, and vocalizations. When fleeing predators, they hold their tail erect — a visible signal to other warthogs in the group. The raised tail is a running alarm signal that communicates alert status without requiring sound. Chemical signals read through smell communicate individual identity and reproductive state between individuals. Body posture signals social rank within the group.

**Response**
When alarmed, warthogs run at speeds up to 55 km/h with the tail held straight up. This raised tail signals alarm to the group as the animal runs. They head for a burrow when one is available — backing into it so the tusks and face face outward toward the entrance. When cornered, they can defend themselves effectively with their tusks. When does remaining near the foraging patch outweigh the growing risk of predator detection in open ground? When is the burrow entrance far enough away that retreating to it costs more energy than it protects? These decisions shape daily activity patterns.

**Learning**
Young warthogs learn foraging areas, routes to water, and responses to threats by staying close to adults and group members. Experience shapes which food patches are most reliable and which movement routes are safest. The group's collective alertness — more eyes and ears scanning from different positions — provides all members with earlier detection of threats than any individual could achieve alone. Spatial memory for reliable food patches and water sources is important in an environment where seasonal availability changes significantly.

**Reproduction**
Females typically give birth to two to four piglets after a gestation of about 170 days. Piglets are born in a burrow. They emerge after about one week and stay close to the mother. The family group shelters together in burrows overnight. This burrow use provides thermal protection against nighttime cold and shelter from nocturnal predators. Sexual maturity occurs at about 18 to 19 months. Multiple litters per year are possible under favorable conditions.

**Constraint**
Body size and limited insulating fat make warthogs vulnerable to cold. They shelter in burrows — often those excavated by aardvarks — to retain heat overnight. Dental wear sets a functional limit on lifespan. The large incisors and molars used for root-digging erode over years of contact with abrasive soil. When the teeth are sufficiently worn, the ability to forage on hard underground food items is compromised. These constraints reflect the specific environment the warthog's body was built for — a warm savanna with soft enough soil for rooting and aardvark-excavated burrows for shelter.

**Cross-Domain Transfer**
Warthogs are ecosystem engineers. Their continuous rooting disturbs and aerates soil, exposing seeds and invertebrates that become available to other species. Their carcasses and dung supply nutrients that cycle through the savanna system. They serve as prey that sustains large African carnivores including lions and leopards. These ecological roles illustrate how a single species can simultaneously shape the physical environment through behavior, supply nutrients through biological cycles, and sustain the populations of predators that in turn regulate the whole community. This multi-role function — one agent simultaneously engineering the environment, cycling nutrients, and feeding predators — is a general principle in ecosystem function and resilient system design.

**Example Answer 1**

**Question:** Why does it run with its tail up?

**Answer:**
When a warthog runs from a predator, it holds its tail straight up in the air. This is a visible alarm signal to other warthogs in the group. Because warthogs often run through tall grass, a raised tail is visible above the grass line even when the body is not. Other warthogs that see the raised tail know to run too, without needing to identify or locate the predator themselves. The raised tail is a group-alert signal transmitted visually during flight.

**Example Answer 2**

**Question:** What are the bumps on its face?

**Answer:**
The bumps — called warts — are thickened pads of skin and connective tissue on the face, more pronounced in males than females. They provide padding that protects the face during fights between males, which involve pressing their faces together and pushing with their tusks. The warts are not actual warts in the medical sense — they are structural features. Their position and size reflect the specific physical interactions warthogs have with each other during social contests.

**Think About This:**
When a body structure is positioned specifically where another animal would hit or apply force, what does that tell you about the social interactions that shaped the design?

---

**Example Answer 3**

**Question:** Why does it back into a burrow?

**Answer:**
Warthogs enter burrows by backing in rather than going in head-first. This positions the face, tusks, and feet facing outward toward the burrow entrance — the direction from which a predator would approach. A warthog in a burrow backed in this way can defend itself with its tusks against an animal trying to enter. If it entered head-first, it would be trapped facing the wrong direction and unable to defend itself. The backing-in behavior is a defensive posture enabled by the burrow structure.

When the geometry of a shelter determines what body orientation allows defense, how does the structure of the environment shape the behavior used within it?
