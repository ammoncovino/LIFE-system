## Screen 2 — Quick Learn

**What can it see, hear, or feel?**
Sheep have wide-set eyes with rectangular horizontal pupils. This gives them a visual field of 270 to 320 degrees — nearly all-around vision without moving their head. Their binocular zone is narrow but enough to judge distance to footing obstacles directly in front. Their Jacobson's organ in the roof of the mouth detects chemical compounds in urine and glandular secretions. This organ plays a central role in reproductive signaling between rams and ewes. Their mobile ear pinnae rotate toward sounds to pinpoint direction.

**Where does it live — and why?**
Domestic sheep are found worldwide in managed settings. Their wild ancestor came from Western Asia. Sheep evolved on grasslands and open pastures where predators could approach from any direction. Their near-panoramic vision and flock response to threat are direct adaptations to open-terrain predation pressure.

**How does it send messages?**
Sheep use bleats and calls to communicate with each other. Each sheep has its own voice and flock members recognize each other by sound. Body posture and proximity signal alertness and social rank within the flock.

**What does it do when something changes?**
When one sheep shows a flight posture, the whole flock reacts immediately. Direction and speed synchronize across the group simultaneously. This is driven by visual contagion — one individual's flight posture triggers matching movement in neighbors. The flock responds as a single coordinated unit without a designated leader.

**How does it learn?**
Sheep learn by watching other flock members. Young sheep follow adults to learn foraging routes and how to respond to threats. Flock membership is the main channel for learning. Sheep have been shown to remember individual faces of other sheep for years.

**How does it have babies?**
Ewes typically give birth to one or two lambs. Gestation lasts about five months. Lambs can stand and walk within hours of birth. They stay close to their mother and flock. Ewes and lambs recognize each other by sound and smell.

**What are its limits?**
Internal parasites — particularly gut nematodes — are a consistent physiological constraint. Sheep have limited behavioral avoidance of contaminated pasture. Wool growth continues independently of season and will mat, accumulate fecal debris, and create heat and parasite risk if not periodically removed.

**What does this teach us?**
A prey animal with eyes on the sides of its head sees the world differently than a predator with eyes in front. The position of the eyes encodes the animal's role in the ecosystem. What an animal can see shapes every decision it makes.

## Screen 3 — Go Deeper

**Perception**
Sheep have wide-set eyes with rectangular horizontal pupils. This produces a visual field of approximately 270 to 320 degrees — near-panoramic surveillance for predators without head movement. Their binocular zone is narrow but sufficient for judging distances to footing obstacles directly in front of the muzzle. The vomeronasal organ — Jacobson's organ — in the roof of the mouth detects non-volatile chemical compounds, particularly those in urine and glandular secretions. This organ plays a central role in reproductive signaling between rams and ewes. Hearing is acute. The mobile ear pinnae rotate toward sound sources to pinpoint direction and distance of disturbances.

**Environment**
Domestic sheep descend from wild sheep native to Western Asia. They were domesticated for wool, milk, and meat and are now found worldwide. Their wild ancestors lived on grasslands, mountain slopes, and open terrain where predators could approach from any direction. Near-panoramic vision and fast synchronized flock response are direct adaptations to open-terrain predation. Sheep graze selectively, cropping grasses and forbs to ground level. This promotes growth of low-growing plants and prevents tall grass species from outcompeting them. Hoof traffic and dung deposition alter soil structure and nutrient cycling across grazed landscapes.

**Signal**
Sheep use bleats and calls to communicate within the flock. Each sheep has an individual voice and flock members recognize each other by sound. Ewes and lambs develop strong mutual recognition through sound and smell within hours of birth. Body posture and proximity signal alertness and social rank. The most powerful signal in the flock is visual contagion: one individual's flight posture triggers immediate matching movement in neighbors. This synchronization does not require a leader or a decision — it propagates through the group automatically.

**Response**
When one sheep shows a flight posture, the whole flock reacts immediately. Direction and speed synchronize simultaneously across the group. This is driven by visual contagion — matching movement propagates through the group as each individual detects the behavior of its nearest neighbors. The flock responds as a single coordinated unit. Resting respiration in sheep averages 12 to 20 breaths per minute, rising sharply during flight and thermal stress. Evaporative water loss through the respiratory tract is the primary cooling mechanism during heat.

**Learning**
Sheep learn by watching other flock members. Young sheep follow adults to learn foraging routes, which plants to eat, and how to respond to threats. Research has shown that sheep can remember individual faces of other sheep for up to two years — a surprisingly long social memory for a prey animal. Flock membership is the main channel through which knowledge and behavioral patterns are transmitted across generations.

**Reproduction**
Ewes typically give birth to one or two lambs. Gestation lasts about five months. Lambs are precocial — they can stand and walk within hours of birth. Ewes and lambs recognize each other by sound and smell within hours of birth. This rapid mutual recognition is important for maintaining mother-offspring bond in a flock where many similar animals are present. Wool growth continues independently of season and must be periodically removed in managed settings.

**Constraint**
Internal parasites — particularly gastrointestinal nematodes — are a consistent physiological constraint. Sheep have limited behavioral avoidance of contaminated pasture. They show lower natural resistance than goats to common parasite species. Wool growth continues independently of season. Without periodic shearing, wool mats, accumulates fecal debris, and creates heat retention and parasite risk. These are costs of the dense, continuous wool growth that evolved for insulation in cold environments.

**Cross-Domain Transfer**
The flock's synchronized flight response — triggered by one individual's posture and propagating through the group without a designated leader — is a model of collective behavior where individual-level rules produce group-level coordination. Each sheep applies a simple rule: if a neighbor shows a flight posture, match it. The result is fast, coordinated group response without communication delay or central decision-making. This principle — simple local rules producing emergent group coordination — is studied in collective intelligence, swarm robotics, and crowd dynamics. The flock is not following a plan. The plan emerges from the interactions.

**Example Answer 1**

**Question:** Why do sheep flock together?

**Answer:**
Flocking reduces each individual's risk from predators in several ways. A predator targeting one sheep in a flock faces a confusion effect — many similar-sized, similarly moving animals make it harder to select and track one target. The flock's combined sensory coverage is greater than any individual's — more eyes and ears scanning means faster threat detection. When one sheep detects danger and reacts, the synchronized flight response means the whole flock moves before most individuals have even detected the original threat. The flock is a collective defense system.

**Think About This:**
If being in a group reduces predation risk, why might a sheep ever separate from the flock — and what would have to make that worth the increased risk?

---

**Example Answer 2**

**Question:** Why does their wool keep growing?

**Answer:**
Domestic sheep have been selectively bred over thousands of years to produce more wool than they naturally shed. Wild sheep have a natural shedding cycle that manages coat length. Domestic breeds have lost or reduced this shedding cycle. The result is continuous wool growth that provides insulation but must be managed — without periodic shearing, wool mats, accumulates debris, retains excessive heat in warm conditions, and can harbor parasites. The continuous growth is a product of domestication, not a feature of the wild ancestor.

**Think About This:**
When humans selectively breed animals for a specific trait, what other changes might come along with that selection — and who is responsible for managing those changes?

---

**Example Answer 3**

**Question:** How do they communicate?

**Answer:**
Sheep communicate through bleats and calls — each individual has a recognizable voice. Ewes and lambs identify each other by sound and smell within hours of birth. The most powerful communication within a flock is visual and behavioral: one sheep's posture or movement triggers matching behavior in neighbors without any vocalization. This visual contagion produces synchronized flock movement. Social rank is communicated through proximity and body posture — higher-ranking animals assert position, and lower-ranking animals yield.

**Think About This:**
When a communication system works by matching behavior rather than exchanging information, what are the advantages of that approach — and what are the limits?
