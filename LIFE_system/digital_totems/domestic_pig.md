## Screen 2 — Quick Learn

**What can it see, hear, or feel?**
Pigs have eyes positioned on the sides of their head. This gives them a visual field of about 310 degrees. They rely heavily on movement cues rather than fine detail. Color vision is limited compared to primates. Their disc-shaped snout is packed with touch sensors that detect texture, temperature, and soil composition while rooting. Their sense of smell is exceptional — pigs can detect odors several meters underground.

**Where does it live — and why?**
Domestic pigs are found worldwide in managed settings. Their wild ancestor is the Eurasian wild boar. Pigs evolved in forest and woodland environments where rooting in soil and leaf litter uncovered food. Their snout and digging behavior reflect this evolutionary history.

**How does it send messages?**
Pigs use a wide range of vocalizations — grunts, squeals, and calls — to communicate with other pigs. Each pig has its own voice and can recognize individuals by sound. Body posture and proximity also signal social rank and intention within the group.

**What does it do when something changes?**
Pigs are alert to changes in sound and smell. When alarmed, they call out and the group responds. They can run at speeds exceeding 18 km/h and are capable swimmers when motivated. Their rooting and foraging behavior changes when the group detects a potential threat.

**How does it learn?**
Pigs are fast learners. They learn from experience and from watching other pigs. Young pigs learn foraging behaviors and social dynamics by staying close to adults. Pigs can learn to associate locations with food and remember those associations well over time.

**How does it have babies?**
A female pig — a sow — typically gives birth to 6 to 12 piglets per litter. Gestation lasts about 114 days. Piglets are born relatively helpless and depend on the sow for warmth and milk. They grow quickly and are weaned within weeks.

**What are its limits?**
Pigs cannot sweat. Without mud, shade, or water for wallowing, body temperature rises rapidly in heat. This is the primary thermal limit. Rooting behavior — while productive — is energetically expensive and becomes restricted when ground is frozen or very hard.

**What does this teach us?**
The pig's snout reveals the relationship between sensory investment and how an animal earns its living. The most developed sensory organ points directly to where the animal finds its food.

## Screen 3 — Go Deeper

**Perception**
Pigs have a visual field of approximately 310 degrees due to laterally placed eyes. This provides broad detection of movement around them while limiting binocular depth perception to a narrow frontal zone. They possess dichromatic color vision and rely heavily on motion cues rather than fine detail. The pig's disc-shaped snout contains a dense concentration of touch sensors that detect texture, temperature, and soil composition during rooting. Olfactory acuity is exceptional. Pigs can detect odors several meters underground and use scent to identify individuals, reproductive state, and feed quality. Hearing is acute and pigs recognize individual voices within their social group.

**Environment**
The wild ancestor of the domestic pig — the Eurasian wild boar — lived in forest and woodland environments across Eurasia. These environments provided leaf litter, soil, roots, tubers, fallen fruit, and invertebrates accessed by rooting. The pig's body — sturdy, low to the ground, with a reinforced snout — reflects this foraging ecology. In semi-feral and feral contexts, pigs act as ecosystem engineers by turning soil through rooting, which alters plant community composition and creates microhabitats used by invertebrates and small vertebrates. Fecal deposition distributes seeds and nutrients across the landscape.

**Signal**
Pigs use a wide range of vocalizations. Each pig has an individual voice and social group members recognize each other by sound. Grunts serve as contact calls within the group. Alarm calls alert others to threats. Body posture and proximity signal social rank and intention. Pig social groups have clear dominance hierarchies. Higher-ranking animals get priority access to food and space. These signals maintain group structure and reduce the frequency of direct physical conflict.

**Response**
When alarmed, pigs call out and the group responds. Their acute sense of smell and hearing alert them to threats before visual detection in forested environments. Despite a heavy-bodied appearance, pigs can run at speeds exceeding 18 km/h and are capable swimmers when motivated. When does proximity to other pigs represent competition for a resource versus shared vigilance against disturbance? This is a recurring decision the animal makes moment by moment.

**Learning**
Pigs learn quickly from experience and from watching other pigs. They can learn to associate locations with food and remember those associations well over time. Young pigs acquire foraging behaviors and social dynamics by staying close to adults. Research in captive settings demonstrates that pigs can learn to use simple tools, recognize themselves in mirrors, and solve multi-step problems — consistent with their relatively large brain size for a domesticated animal.

**Reproduction**
A sow typically gives birth to 6 to 12 piglets per litter. Gestation lasts about 114 days. Piglets are born relatively helpless and depend on the sow for warmth and milk. They grow quickly and are weaned within weeks. Pigs reach sexual maturity at about 6 to 8 months. Multiple litters per year are possible, giving pigs a relatively fast reproductive rate among large domesticated animals.

**Constraint**
The absence of functional sweat glands creates a hard thermal ceiling. Body temperature rises rapidly in heat without access to mud, shade, or water for wallowing. Pigs cool themselves by spreading moisture on their skin from external sources — not from internal sweating. Rooting behavior, while metabolically productive, is energetically expensive and becomes restricted when ground freezes or hardens. These constraints reflect the temperate forest and woodland environments the pig's body was built for.

**Cross-Domain Transfer**
The pig's snout reveals the relationship between sensory investment and foraging ecology. The most developed sensory organ points directly to where the animal finds its food — buried roots, tubers, and invertebrates in soil. In semi-feral and feral contexts, pigs alter the physical environment through rooting: turning soil, exposing seeds and invertebrates, and creating microhabitats used by other species. This ecosystem engineering effect — one species reshaping the physical conditions for others through its normal behavior — is a general principle in ecology, urban planning, and organizational systems. Where resources flow through a system shapes everything downstream.

**Example Answer 1**

**Question:** Why does it roll in mud?

**Answer:**
Pigs lack functional sweat glands — they cannot cool themselves by sweating the way humans or horses can. Without the ability to evaporate moisture from their own skin, pigs must acquire moisture from outside sources. Mud provides a wet, evaporative cooling surface that absorbs and slowly releases heat. It also coats the skin, protecting against sunburn and insect bites. Rolling in mud is a thermoregulatory and protective behavior, not a hygiene preference.

**Example Answer 2**

**Question:** How smart is it?

**Answer:**
Pigs have large brains relative to their body size among domestic animals. Research in captive settings demonstrates that pigs can learn to use simple tools, solve multi-step problems, and show long-term spatial memory for food locations. They recognize individual voices within their social group and can learn to associate symbols with objects. Their cognitive profile is consistent with an omnivore that forages in complex, variable environments where flexible problem-solving provides an advantage.

**Example Answer 3**

**Question:** Why does it root in the ground?

**Answer:**
Pigs use their reinforced disc-shaped snout to push into soil and turn it over. This rooting behavior locates buried roots, tubers, invertebrates, and fungi that are nutritionally dense food sources. The snout is packed with touch sensors that detect texture, temperature, and soil composition — it is a highly sensitive detection and excavation tool in one. The pig's digestive system processes both plant and animal material efficiently, making the wide range of buried food items accessible and worth the energy cost of rooting.
