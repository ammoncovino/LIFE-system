## Screen 2 — Quick Learn

**What can it see, hear, or feel?**
Cownose rays have a sense humans do not have at all. They can detect tiny electrical fields made by living things. This lets them find clams and oysters buried in sand where nothing can be seen or smelled. They also use smell, pressure sensing through their lateral line system, and sight.

**Where does it live — and why?**
It lives in the western Atlantic Ocean and Gulf of Mexico. It moves from shallow coastal bays to open water. It migrates by season — north in spring, south in fall — following warm water and food. It needs sandy or muddy bottom habitat where buried shellfish can be found.

**How does it send messages?**
Rays communicate mainly through body position and closeness within the school. They produce low-frequency pressure waves through their movement. Nearby individuals detect these waves. Chemical signals in the water may also carry information about breeding during mating season.

**What does it do when something changes?**
In a school, one ray's change in direction spreads through the whole group fast. The school responds as one unit without any leader. Alone, a ray may speed up and turn away from large objects or sharp stimuli.

**How does it learn?**
Rays can learn from experience. They remember good feeding spots and return to them. Young rays travel with adults, which may show them productive feeding areas and seasonal travel routes.

**How does it have babies?**
Cownose rays give birth to live pups. A female carries one pup inside her for about 11 to 12 months and feeds it through a placenta-like structure. The pup is born fully formed and can swim right away.

**What are its limits?**
It needs water temperature within a specific range — cold water triggers migration. It needs sandy or muddy bottom for feeding. Its tooth plates work on shellfish but not on all foods. Large sharks are its main predators.

**What does this teach us?**
An animal can sense a whole type of information — electrical fields — that is completely missing from human experience. The world holds more information than any one type of sensor can detect.

## Screen 3 — Go Deeper

**Perception**
Cownose rays have a special sense called electroreception. A network of tiny organs called the ampullae of Lorenzini — gel-filled pores clustered on the snout — detect electrical signals as small as a few nanovolts per centimeter. These organs detect the bioelectric fields made by the muscle movement and nerve activity of prey animals buried in sand. No eyes or nose could find them there. The lateral line system — pressure sensors along the body surface — detects water movement made by nearby objects. Vision works but the eyes sit on the sides and top of the head. This suggests the eyes are mainly for watching out for threats, not for pinpointing food. Smell is well-developed and likely helps detect chemicals linked to prey and other rays.

**Environment**
The cownose ray lives in the western Atlantic from southern New England to Brazil, including the Gulf of Mexico and Caribbean Sea. It uses many marine environments — from shallow bays to open ocean during migration. It is strongly connected to sandy and muddy seafloor habitats with populations of clams, oysters, and other shellfish. These make up the main diet. Seasonal migration is well-documented: large schools move north along the US Atlantic coast in spring and back south in fall, following water temperatures between about 15 and 30 degrees Celsius. Schools of thousands travel together during peak migration.

**Signal**
Social communication in cownose rays is less well-studied than in animals that call out loud. Inside schools, hydrodynamic signaling — pressure waves and water movement from neighboring rays swimming — likely coordinates turning and speed changes. During breeding season, males follow females closely, tracking chemical signals in the water. Courtship includes males biting the female's pectoral fin — a physical contact that starts mating. The school itself works as a group signal: a school of thousands is much harder for a predator to target than a single ray.

**Response**
One ray changing speed or direction sharply makes neighbors respond within milliseconds. This cascade spreads through the school without each individual needing to detect the original threat. This creates the wave-like turning pattern seen in large schools of fish and rays. Individual rays may also breach — jumping partially or fully out of the water — during large-school migration. Why they do this is not fully understood but may relate to removing parasites or sending social signals.

**Learning**
Sharks and rays can learn to connect specific locations or signals with food rewards in lab settings. They remember this information over time. In wild cownose rays, knowing seasonal migration routes and good feeding areas likely builds up through experience and by following other rays. Young rays travel in large schools that include experienced adults. This provides a chance to learn routes and habitat knowledge by following along.

**Reproduction**
Cownose rays give birth to a single pup that develops inside the mother. After the yolk runs out, the pup is fed by secretions from the wall of the uterus. Pregnancy lasts about 11 to 12 months. At birth, the pup is fully formed, able to swim, and able to find food using electroreception. Sexual maturity comes at about 4 to 5 years old. Low reproductive output — one pup per year per female — means populations recover slowly from decline. The pup is independent immediately after birth but receives no parental guidance after that.

**Constraint**
Cownose rays cannot stay in water below about 12 to 15 degrees Celsius. They move seasonally to stay in their temperature range. Their flat body and fin-based swimming is built for open-water cruising, not tight maneuvering in small spaces. Prey access depends on having sandy or muddy seafloor. Hard rock bottoms hold no buried shellfish. The tooth plates work well on bivalves but not on all food types. Their high mobility and schooling protect them from predators but also gather the population in predictable places during migration.

**Cross-Domain Transfer**
The ampullae of Lorenzini detect a type of signal — electric fields — that is completely outside human sensing range. This system reveals a class of environmental information that exists in water and some land environments but is invisible to any animal without the right detector. In engineering, detecting electromagnetic fields is the basis for technologies from power grid monitoring to medical brain scans (EEG) and heart scans (ECG). The core idea — that weak field gradients carry location and activity information about their source — is the same idea those medical tools use. Think of it this way: the ray solved the problem of finding buried prey with no visual or smell clues by evolving a detector tuned to the prey's own electrical output.

**Example Answer 1**

**Question:** How does it find food it can't see?

**Answer:**
Cownose rays find buried clams and oysters using electroreception. Small sense organs called the ampullae of Lorenzini are clustered on the snout. They detect weak electrical fields. Every living animal produces a small electrical field from its muscle and nerve activity. Clams buried in sand are invisible and nearly odorless. But they produce a bioelectric signal the ray's sensors can detect from a short distance. The ray reads the electrical output of a buried animal as a location signal.

**Example Answer 2**

**Question:** Does it sting?

**Answer:**
Cownose rays have a spine at the base of the tail that contains venom. It can cause a painful wound if the ray is stepped on or held down. The spine is a defense — the ray does not use it to attack on purpose. In touch pool settings, cownose rays in managed facilities typically have their spines trimmed to reduce risk. Spines grow back and the trimming must be repeated. Following facility guidelines and observing from outside the pool is the right approach.

**Think About This:**
When an animal has a defensive structure, what does its presence tell you about the threats that animal faces in its natural environment?

---

**Example Answer 3**

**Question:** Why does it swim in a school?

**Answer:**
Schools of cownose rays — sometimes thousands of them — provide collective benefits. Each ray's senses cover a limited area. The school's combined coverage is far larger than any one ray's. A predator targeting one ray in a school faces a confusion problem — many similar-sized, similarly moving animals make it hard to pick and track one target. During migration, following the school also provides direction information. The school is a group structure that gives each individual a survival benefit — no central coordinator required.

**Think About This:**
A school has no leader and no plan, yet it moves as a coordinated unit. What does that tell you about how order can come from a system without any central controller?
