OSTRICH
*Struthio camelus*

Order: Struthioniformes
Native to: Africa
Diet: Omnivore (plants, seeds, insects, small vertebrates)
Respiration: Lungs

1. What does it see?
The ostrich has the largest eyes of any living terrestrial vertebrate — each eye measures approximately 5 cm in diameter — providing exceptional acuity and the ability to detect moving objects at distances exceeding 3.5 km across open savanna. Eyes are positioned laterally, but the bird can swivel its long neck to bring either eye to bear on a target with precision.

2. What does it sense?
Hearing is acute; the large, mobile ear openings (lacking external pinnae) funnel sound directly to the tympanic membrane, enabling detection of low-frequency calls from other ostriches at long range. Tactile sensitivity in the bill tip allows discrimination of seed size, texture, and surface hardness during foraging on mixed ground substrate.

3. How does it move?
Ostriches are the fastest running birds on Earth, capable of sustained speeds of 45–50 km/h and short bursts exceeding 70 km/h, powered by large leg muscles anchored to a reduced but massive pelvic girdle. They are the only birds with two toes; the large inner toe bears the full weight and carries a blunt nail used as a defensive weapon capable of delivering fatal kicks.

4. How does it breathe?
Ostriches use the avian flow-through respiratory system with air sacs acting as bellows; at running speed, each stride compresses and expands the air sac system, creating mechanical coupling between locomotion and ventilation that reduces the energy cost of breathing during sustained running. During heat, they raise their feathers to expose the less-insulated skin of the inner thigh, increasing radiative and convective heat loss.

5. What fuels it?
Ostriches consume a wide variety of plant parts — seeds, succulents, grasses — along with insects, lizards, and small vertebrates when encountered; no food item is actively stalked but opportunistically taken during movement. A handful of pellets plus access to grasses, a few seeds, and occasional animal protein pieces supports the daily energy budget of a captive ostrich.

6. What limits it?
Ostriches swallow grit and stones deliberately to assist the gizzard in mechanically grinding hard seeds; without access to grit, digestive efficiency drops substantially. Their reproductive cycle is tightly coupled to thermoregulation: nest incubation on open ground exposes both eggs and incubating adults to sustained heat loading, which limits nesting success in extreme temperatures.

7. What decision is constant?
When does the combined vigilance of multiple ostriches in a group reduce individual predator monitoring enough to allow longer uninterrupted feeding bouts? When does the approach of an unfamiliar large animal require a kick, a display, or flight as the lower-cost response?

8. What role does it play?
Ostriches are bulk seed dispersers and disturb soil through scratching and incidental digging, creating depressions that collect water and support localized plant germination after rains. As large-bodied prey, they sustain populations of lions, cheetahs, and spotted hyenas that regulate the broader herbivore community.
