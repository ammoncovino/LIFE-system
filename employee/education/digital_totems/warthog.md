WARTHOG
*Phacochoerus africanus*

Order: Artiodactyla
Native to: Sub-Saharan Africa
Diet: Omnivore (grasses, roots, bark, insects)
Respiration: Lungs

1. What does it see?
Warthogs have eyes positioned high and laterally on the skull, providing a wide field of view that allows detection of predators from nearly all directions while grazing with the head down. Their color vision is limited compared to primates, but motion detection in peripheral vision is acute.

2. What does it sense?
The warthog's large, mobile ears rotate independently to localize sounds, detecting approach from predators even in tall grass. A well-developed sense of smell is used to locate underground food sources and to read chemical signals left by other warthogs.

3. How does it move?
Warthogs move on their wrist pads when rooting and foraging, bearing weight on the carpal joints rather than the hooves. When fleeing predators, they can sustain speeds up to 55 km/h, holding the tail erect as a visible signal to other warthogs in the group.

4. How does it breathe?
Lungs draw air through the nostrils, which are reinforced by the cartilaginous warthog snout built for rooting in compacted soil. At high locomotor speeds, respiratory rate increases sharply to meet the elevated oxygen demand of the running muscles.

5. What fuels it?
Grasses and grass rhizomes form the bulk of the diet in the wet season, while roots, bulbs, and bark become primary energy sources during dry months when grasses die back. Insects and carrion are consumed opportunistically, contributing protein when plant material is nutritionally lean.

6. What limits it?
Body size and limited insulating fat make warthogs vulnerable to cold; they shelter in burrows (often those excavated by aardvarks) to retain heat overnight. Dental wear sets a functional limit on lifespan, as the large incisors and molars used for root-digging erode over years of abrasive soil contact.

7. What decision is constant?
When does remaining near the foraging patch outweigh the growing risk of predator detection in open ground? When is the burrow entrance far enough away that retreating to it costs more energy than it protects?

8. What role does it play?
Warthogs disturb and aerate soil through continuous rooting, exposing seeds and invertebrates that become available to other species. Their carcasses and dung supply nutrients that cycle through the savanna system, and they serve as prey that sustains large African carnivores including lions and leopards.
