## Screen 2 — Quick Learn

**What can it see, hear, or feel?**
Ring-tailed lemurs have sharp color vision and see well in daylight. They also rely heavily on scent — their noses detect information that eyes cannot. They hear calls from far away and use their ears to track group members.

**Where does it live — and why?**
It lives in dry forests, scrubland, and forest edges in southern Madagascar. This environment has open ground and trees, and the lemur uses both layers — walking on the ground and climbing into branches to feed or watch for danger.

**How does it send messages?**
It uses scent markings left on branches and rocks, loud group calls, and body postures. Males have scent glands on their wrists and chest and use them to mark objects during disputes.

**What does it do when something changes?**
It scans for threats, calls to group members, and may move quickly to a safer location. Groups respond to alarm calls from individuals — one animal detecting danger alerts the whole group.

**How does it learn?**
Young lemurs watch adults and repeat behaviors — which foods to eat, where to move, and how to respond to other animals. Social learning within the group is the main channel for passing down knowledge.

**How does it have babies?**
Females give birth once a year, usually to one or two infants. Infants cling to the mother's belly at first and later ride on her back. They stay close to the mother and group for several months.

**What are its limits?**
It needs specific vegetation for food and cover. It is sensitive to temperature and requires the sun to warm its body in the morning — a behavior called sunning. It cannot survive without a stable social group.

**What does this teach us?**
A social animal's survival depends not just on its own senses but on the combined sensing of the group. Information moves through a group, not just through an individual.

## Screen 3 — Go Deeper

**Perception**
Ring-tailed lemurs possess trichromatic color vision — they distinguish red, green, and blue wavelengths — which helps them identify ripe fruit. Their eyes are positioned to give a wide field of view with some binocular overlap, useful for both spotting predators and navigating branches. Olfactory perception is highly developed: they have a large nasal surface area and a functioning vomeronasal organ, which processes chemical signals that carry identity, reproductive status, and territorial information. Hearing is well-developed, tuned to the frequency range of their own vocalizations and those of predators.

**Environment**
This species occupies the dry deciduous forests, spiny thicket, and gallery forests of southern and southwestern Madagascar. The region is marked by seasonal drought, and ring-tailed lemurs have adapted to fluctuating food availability. Unlike most lemur species, they spend significant time on the ground — foraging, traveling, and sunning — which exposes them to both terrestrial and arboreal threats. Their habitat includes multiple vertical layers, and they use all of them depending on food source, temperature, and threat level.

**Signal**
Ring-tailed lemurs use a multimodal signaling system. Scent is the primary channel for territorial marking and individual identity: males engage in "stink fights," rubbing scent from wrist glands onto their tails and waving them toward rivals. Vocal signals include alarm calls, contact calls, and infant calls, each with distinct acoustic structure. Body posture — including tail position and orientation — carries social meaning within the group. A signal only functions when the receiver can detect and interpret it; both sender and receiver systems must be compatible.

**Response**
Behavioral responses depend on threat type, distance, and intensity. Aerial predator alarm calls trigger upward scanning and movement toward cover. Terrestrial predator alarms produce different vocalizations and ground-level escape patterns. The group does not respond uniformly — individuals closer to the stimulus respond first, and response propagates through the group as secondary individuals detect the first animal's reaction. This is a cascade response system dependent on both direct perception and social cue detection.

**Learning**
Ring-tailed lemur infants begin observational learning from birth, watching what the mother eats, avoids, and how she responds to stimuli. Diet breadth is partly socially transmitted: juveniles sample foods their mothers and group members handle before eating them independently. Spatial knowledge of food patches and water sources is also accumulated through group movement and appears to be retained across seasons. Social play in juveniles provides a lower-stakes context for practicing competitive and navigational behaviors.

**Reproduction**
Breeding is seasonal, triggered by photoperiod changes. Females cycle in April–June and give birth approximately four and a half months later. Litter size is typically one to two. Infants are born with open eyes and are precocial compared to many mammals but still require extended maternal investment. Females are the dominant sex and control access to resources and mates. Social stability in the group directly affects infant survival rates.

**Constraint**
Ring-tailed lemurs are constrained by habitat specificity: they require areas with both ground access and trees, native vegetation for food, and a seasonal climate their physiology is calibrated to. They cannot internally regulate body temperature as efficiently as many mammals, which is why they sun themselves each morning — pressing their body toward heat sources to passively warm up. Population size is constrained by group social dynamics: groups too small to collectively detect threats have reduced survival, while groups too large compete for food internally.

**Cross-Domain Transfer**
The ring-tailed lemur's alarm call system illustrates how information can be distributed across a network rather than stored in one individual. Each animal is a sensor node. The group's collective ability to detect threats exceeds any single individual's capacity. This principle — distributed sensing producing emergent group awareness — appears in engineered sensor networks, organizational safety systems, and collective intelligence research. The study of how signals propagate through animal groups has directly informed models of distributed alarm systems.

**Example Answer 1**

**Question:** Why do they all face the sun in the morning?

**Answer:**
Ring-tailed lemurs cannot generate enough body heat on their own in cool temperatures. In the morning, they sit facing the sun and spread their arms to expose as much skin surface as possible to solar heat. This is passive thermoregulation — using the environment to manage body temperature rather than internal metabolism alone.

**Think About This:**

**Example Answer 2**

**Question:** What does their tail do?

**Answer:**
The black-and-white banded tail serves as a visual signal. In dense vegetation, a raised tail makes an individual easier to spot and helps the group stay together while moving. During scent conflicts, males rub scent onto their tails and wave them toward rivals. The tail is a communication tool shaped by the animal's visual and chemical signaling systems.

**Think About This:**
A single body structure doing multiple jobs at once — what does that tell you about how biological systems are built?

---

**Example Answer 3**

**Question:** How does the group decide where to go?

**Answer:**
Movement in ring-tailed lemur groups is not coordinated by a single decision-maker. Dominant females tend to initiate movement, and other individuals follow. When multiple individuals move in different directions, the group tends to follow the direction chosen by most. Research on lemur movement suggests group decisions emerge from the sum of individual choices rather than from one leader giving instructions.
