DOMESTIC GOAT
*Capra aegagrus hircus*

Order: Artiodactyla
Native to: Western Asia (domesticated worldwide)
Diet: Herbivore (browse, grasses, bark)
Respiration: Lungs

1. What does it see?
Goats have rectangular, horizontal pupils that expand their visual field to nearly 320 degrees, enabling detection of predator movement across open terrain while the head is lowered to feed. This pupil shape provides a wide depth of field across the horizontal plane, keeping more of the visual environment in focus simultaneously.

2. What does it sense?
The goat's upper lip is split and highly mobile, functioning as a tactile organ that sorts plant material by texture and moisture before it reaches the molars. Scent glands located below the eyes (preorbital), on the feet (interdigital), and near the tail produce chemical signals used in individual recognition and reproductive communication.

3. How does it move?
Hooves are split into two toes with hard outer walls and softer inner pads that grip irregular surfaces, allowing goats to stand on rocky slopes and narrow ledges. The skeletal structure of the carpals and tarsals allows independent articulation of each digit, enabling precise foot placement on uneven terrain.

4. How does it breathe?
Air moves through the lungs via a diaphragm-driven bellows system; the respiratory rate increases during exertion on steep terrain. Goats do not pant as a primary cooling strategy; they use shade-seeking and reduced activity during peak heat, supplemented by evaporative cooling through skin and respiratory surfaces.

5. What fuels it?
As ruminants, goats ferment plant material in a four-chambered stomach, extracting nutrients from fibrous browse, bark, and coarse grasses that monogastric animals cannot efficiently process. Mulberry leaves, local browse, and woody stems are preferred over uniform pasture grasses, reflecting an evolutionary history in scrubby, varied terrain.

6. What limits it?
Copper sensitivity creates a narrow dietary tolerance; excess copper in feed quickly accumulates to toxic liver levels, making mineral balance a continuous constraint in managed care. Wet conditions sustained over time predispose hooves to rot and foot infections, limiting activity and food intake on waterlogged ground.

7. What decision is constant?
When does the energy available from the current browse patch justify continued stripping of bark, and when does moving to a new stem become more efficient? When does the height advantage of climbing a structure produce enough new forage to offset the energy of the climb?

8. What role does it play?
In natural and feral settings, goats function as browsers that suppress woody shrub encroachment, opening habitat structure for grasses and low-growing plants used by other species. Selective removal of plant tissue influences canopy composition and light penetration at the ground layer.
