DOMESTIC SHEEP
*Ovis aries*

Order: Artiodactyla
Native to: Western Asia (domesticated worldwide)
Diet: Herbivore (grasses, forbs)
Respiration: Lungs

1. What does it see?
Sheep possess wide-set eyes with rectangular horizontal pupils, producing a visual field of approximately 270–320 degrees that enables near-panoramic surveillance for predators without head movement. Their binocular zone is narrow but sufficient for judging distances to footing obstacles directly in front of the muzzle.

2. What does it sense?
The sheep's vomeronasal organ (Jacobson's organ) in the roof of the mouth detects non-volatile chemical compounds, particularly those in urine and glandular secretions, and plays a central role in reproductive signaling between rams and ewes. Hearing is acute; the mobile ear pinnae rotate toward sound sources to pinpoint direction and distance of disturbances.

3. How does it move?
Sheep move on cloven hooves with hard keratin walls and softer digital pads, providing grip on uneven pasture ground. When alarmed, the flock synchronizes direction and speed simultaneously — a behavior driven by visual contagion where one individual's flight posture triggers immediate matching movement in neighbors.

4. How does it breathe?
Resting respiration in sheep averages 12–20 breaths per minute, rising sharply during flight and thermal stress. Evaporative water loss through the respiratory tract is the primary cooling mechanism during heat; wool insulation, while thermally protective in cold, creates heat retention challenges in warm climates without shearing.

5. What fuels it?
Sheep are ruminants with a four-chambered stomach; ingested grasses and forbs are fermented by microbial populations in the rumen before the resulting volatile fatty acids are absorbed as the primary energy source. A handful of hay or pasture grass per feeding session supplies the fibrous substrate that keeps rumen fermentation active and gut motility regular.

6. What limits it?
Internal parasites — particularly gastrointestinal nematodes — are a consistent physiological constraint, as sheep have limited behavioral avoidance of contaminated pasture and show lower natural resistance than goats. Wool growth continues independently of season and will mat, accumulate fecal debris, and create heat and parasite risk if not periodically removed.

7. What decision is constant?
When does the safety of remaining with the flock outweigh moving toward a more productive patch of forage separated from the group? When does the time spent at the current grazing site produce diminishing returns relative to the risk of moving to unfamiliar ground?

8. What role does it play?
Grazing sheep selectively crop grasses and forbs to ground level, promoting growth of low-growing plants and preventing tall grass species from outcompeting them. Hoof traffic and dung deposition together alter soil structure and nutrient cycling across grazed landscapes.
