DOMESTIC PIG
*Sus scrofa domesticus*

Order: Artiodactyla
Native to: Eurasia (domesticated worldwide)
Diet: Omnivore (grains, vegetation, roots)
Respiration: Lungs

1. What does it see?
Pigs have a visual field of approximately 310 degrees due to laterally placed eyes, providing broad detection of movement around them while limiting binocular depth perception to a narrow frontal zone. They possess dichromatic color vision and rely heavily on motion cues rather than fine detail.

2. What does it sense?
The pig's disc-shaped snout contains a dense concentration of tactile mechanoreceptors (Eimer's organs) that detect texture, temperature, and soil composition during rooting. Olfactory acuity is exceptional; pigs can detect odors several meters underground and use scent to identify individuals, reproductive state, and feed quality.

3. How does it move?
Pigs walk on two of their four toes (digits three and four), with the lateral digits making only light contact with the ground on soft surfaces. Despite a heavy-bodied appearance, they can run at speeds exceeding 18 km/h and are capable swimmers when motivated.

4. How does it breathe?
Lungs expand and contract via a muscular diaphragm; the snout acts as the primary air-intake structure, filtering particles during rooting. Thermoregulation is constrained because pigs lack functional sweat glands; panting and wallowing in mud or water serve as the primary mechanisms for heat dissipation.

5. What fuels it?
A digestive system similar to the human monogastric gut allows pigs to extract energy efficiently from starchy grains and diverse plant material. A handful of pellets and root vegetables supply the carbohydrate base, while protein requirements are met through legume meal, insects, or animal byproducts in managed diets.

6. What limits it?
The absence of sweat glands creates a hard thermal ceiling; body temperature rises rapidly in heat without access to mud, shade, or water for wallowing. Rooting behavior, while metabolically productive, is energetically expensive and becomes restricted when ground freezes or hardens.

7. What decision is constant?
When does the energy cost of rooting deeper into harder substrate exceed the caloric value of what is likely to be found? When does proximity to other pigs represent competition for a resource versus shared vigilance against disturbance?

8. What role does it play?
In semi-feral and feral contexts, pigs act as ecosystem engineers by turning soil through rooting, which alters plant community composition and creates microhabitats used by invertebrates and small vertebrates. Fecal deposition distributes seeds and nutrients across the landscape, cycling organic matter through the soil system.
