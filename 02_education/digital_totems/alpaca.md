ALPACA
*Vicugna pacos*

Order: Artiodactyla
Native to: South America (Andes)
Diet: Herbivore (grasses, hay)
Respiration: Lungs

1. What does it see?
Alpacas have laterally placed eyes with a wide visual field exceeding 300 degrees, adapted to detecting movement across open Andean plateaus where visual horizons are broad and predators approach from distance. The pupils are elongated horizontally, expanding peripheral depth of field while the animal grazes with its head lowered.

2. What does it sense?
Alpacas use a highly mobile upper lip to sort forage by texture before ingestion, and they investigate unfamiliar objects primarily through close olfactory inspection. Ear movement is pronounced and independent; rotating pinnae allow localization of sound sources, and ear position communicates social status and alertness state within the herd.

3. How does it move?
Alpacas move on padded two-toed feet; unlike sheep and goats, their feet have a leathery sole pad rather than hard hooves, which causes less soil disturbance but limits traction on steep wet rock. They walk and trot using a lateral gait (moving both limbs on the same side together), which is efficient for sustained travel over high-altitude uneven terrain.

4. How does it breathe?
Alpacas evolved at altitudes of 3,500–5,000 meters and have red blood cells with an unusually high hemoglobin oxygen affinity, allowing efficient oxygen extraction from thin, high-altitude air. At lower elevations, this same physiology results in exceptional respiratory efficiency during exertion, with respiratory rates recovering quickly after movement.

5. What fuels it?
As modified ruminants (camelids have a three-compartment stomach rather than four), alpacas ferment plant material highly efficiently and extract more nutrition per unit of forage than sheep or goats, making them well-suited to sparse, low-quality Andean grasses and hay. A handful of grass hay twice daily provides the fiber base needed for rumen fermentation, supplemented by mineral access.

6. What limits it?
Dental malocclusion is a recurring constraint; alpaca incisors grow continuously and misalignment reduces foraging efficiency over time, requiring managed trimming in captive settings. Heat stress is a significant risk at lower altitudes, as the dense fiber coat adapted for Andean cold retains heat in warm humid environments without shade and water access.

7. What decision is constant?
When does the proximity of an unfamiliar animal or object cross a threshold that triggers alarm calling and group movement away? When does returning to a depleted patch of hay represent less energy cost than competing for a fuller one occupied by a dominant herd member?

8. What role does it play?
In Andean grassland ecosystems, alpacas and their wild ancestor the vicuña graze selectively in ways that can maintain grass diversity if stocking density stays within carrying capacity. Dung is concentrated in communal latrine areas, creating localized nutrient hotspots that differ chemically from surrounding grazed soil.
