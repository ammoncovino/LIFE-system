SLUG: black-and-white-ruffed-lemur
NAME: BLACK-AND-WHITE RUFFED LEMUR
SCI: Varecia variegata
WHERE: Madagascar — tropical rainforest canopy
CONS: Critically Endangered

Q1 sense:
  - It uses smell to find ripe fruit and to identify individual group members.
  - Its loud calls carry far through the forest and it listens for responses.
  - Its eyes face forward for precise depth perception while leaping between trees.
  - It sniffs branches to read scent marks left by other lemurs.
Q2 habitat:
  - It lives in the mature rainforest canopy of eastern Madagascar.
  - It forages mainly in the upper levels of tall, fruit-bearing trees.
  - Dense forest provides escape routes from aerial and ground predators.
  - A large home range is needed because fruiting trees are scattered across the forest.
Q3 signals:
  - Its call is one of the loudest produced by any primate.
  - This call warns the group of predators and tells neighbors a territory is occupied.
  - Males scent-mark by lowering their chin and chest onto a branch.
  - Females mark territory by rubbing the anogenital region on horizontal surfaces.
Q4 change:
  - It gives loud alarm calls immediately when it detects a predator.
  - After an alarm, the group moves into denser foliage and becomes quieter.
  - Scent marking increases during the breeding season to communicate readiness.
  - It avoids areas of the forest that have been disturbed or cleared.
Q5 learn:
  - Infants stay in nests early in life and gradually follow adults into the canopy.
  - Young lemurs learn fruiting tree locations by traveling with adult group members.
  - They learn scent-marking behavior by watching adults from about five months of age.
Q6 babies:
  - The female builds up to fifteen nests before giving birth to her young.
  - Two to six infants are born after about 102 days of gestation.
  - Infants cannot cling and remain in the nest for two to three weeks.
  - Related and unrelated group members help guard nests while the mother forages.
Q7 limits:
  - Heavy deforestation in Madagascar has removed most of its forest habitat.
  - Small, isolated forest patches cannot support groups that need large fruiting ranges.
  - Even its faster reproduction rate cannot offset the pace of habitat loss.
  - It is Critically Endangered due to ongoing deforestation and hunting.
Q8 teaches:
  - Building fifteen nests before birth protects young from canopy dangers.
  - Shared nest-guarding across the group helps more infants survive.
  - Its calls carry farther through the forest than any other lemur's.
