SLUG: linnes-two-toed-sloth
NAME: LINNAEUS'S TWO-TOED SLOTH
SCI: Choloepus didactylus
WHERE: South America — tropical rainforest canopy
CONS: Least concern

Q1 sense:
  - Its hearing is keen and may help it sense movement in the canopy.
  - Its eyesight is weak but can detect shapes and light changes.
  - Its nose detects food and other sloths through smell.
  - Its curved claws feel the tension and texture of each branch it grips.
Q2 habitat:
  - It spends almost its entire life hanging upside down in the rainforest canopy.
  - Dense tree cover provides both food and camouflage from eagles and ocelots.
  - It descends to the ground only once a week to defecate.
  - Green algae that grows in its fur helps it blend into leafy branches.
Q3 signals:
  - It is mostly silent but hisses and moans when threatened.
  - It slashes with its long claws as a defense when grabbed.
  - Stillness itself is a signal — staying motionless is how it avoids detection.
  - A curled body position in the branches signals that it is resting safely.
Q4 change:
  - When disturbed, it moves slowly to a denser cluster of branches.
  - It will bite and claw if it cannot escape a predator.
  - Louder sounds cause it to slow its movements further or stay completely still.
  - It changes trees when its food source in one tree is used up.
Q5 learn:
  - A young sloth clings to its mother for up to a year after birth.
  - It learns which trees and leaves to eat by riding with its mother.
  - Young sloths gradually explore nearby branches while staying within the mother's range.
Q6 babies:
  - A female gives birth to a single young after about ten months of gestation.
  - The infant clings to the mother's belly and is carried everywhere she goes.
  - The mother gives birth while hanging upside down in the canopy.
  - Young sloths cling tightly and learn the canopy slowly over many months.
Q7 limits:
  - Its very low metabolic rate means it produces little body heat.
  - On the ground it is nearly helpless against predators.
  - Loss of rainforest trees removes both its food and its hiding places.
  - Very loud disturbances can disrupt its activity and foraging patterns.
Q8 teaches:
  - Being slow and still is a survival strategy, not a weakness.
  - Its fur, posture, and algae growth all work together to hide it from predators.
  - A sloth's survival depends on a whole forest — not just one branch.
