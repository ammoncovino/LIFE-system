SLUG: prehensile-tailed-porcupine
NAME: PREHENSILE-TAILED PORCUPINE
SCI: Coendou prehensilis
WHERE: South America — tropical and subtropical rainforest canopy
CONS: Least concern

Q1 sense:
  - It relies mainly on smell and touch to move through the dark canopy.
  - Long whiskers feel branches and gaps in the canopy at night.
  - Its nose detects food and the scent marks of other porcupines on branches.
  - Its hearing picks up sounds of predators and unfamiliar movement below.
Q2 habitat:
  - It spends over 85 percent of its time in the rainforest canopy.
  - It sleeps in high branches, tree forks, or hollow trunks during the day.
  - It eats leaves, bark, roots, and fruit found in the trees.
  - It rarely touches the ground, moving between trees by reaching from branch to branch.
Q3 signals:
  - It raises its quills perpendicular to its body when threatened.
  - It turns sideways to look larger in front of an approaching predator.
  - It moans, grunts, hisses, and chatters to communicate with other porcupines.
  - Scent from flank glands marks the branches and trees it uses regularly.
Q4 change:
  - During the day it stays still and hidden in the tree canopy.
  - It wakes at dusk and moves to a different tree each night.
  - When threatened, it stands on its hind legs and growls before using its quills.
  - It curls its body to protect its soft underside from predators.
Q5 learn:
  - The single young stays near its mother and learns which trees to visit.
  - It is born with open eyes and climbs trees within a few days.
  - It gradually learns the canopy routes the mother uses before foraging independently.
Q6 babies:
  - After about 203 days, the female gives birth to one large young.
  - The newborn has soft, flexible spines that harden within hours after birth.
  - It is born with its eyes open and can grip branches almost immediately.
  - The young porcupine nurses from the mother while also beginning to try solid food.
Q7 limits:
  - It cannot jump — it must physically reach each new tree by branch.
  - Loss of canopy cover cuts off its travel routes and food sources.
  - Daylight activity is almost entirely absent — exposure during the day increases predation risk.
  - Jaguars hunt it on the rare occasions it touches the ground.
Q8 teaches:
  - Never needing the ground, it is shaped entirely by the canopy above.
  - Its quills, scent glands, and nocturnal timing are three different tools for staying safe.
  - It drops seeds and gnaws bark, shaping the forest as it feeds.
