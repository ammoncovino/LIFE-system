SLUG: geoffroys-spider-monkey
NAME: GEOFFROY'S SPIDER MONKEY
SCI: Ateles geoffroyi
WHERE: Central and South America — tropical rainforest canopy
CONS: Endangered

Q1 sense:
  - Its eyes face forward, giving it sharp depth perception for jumping between trees.
  - It hears calls from group members hundreds of meters away.
  - It smells fruit to judge whether it is ripe before picking it.
  - Its prehensile tail and long fingers feel the strength of each branch.
Q2 habitat:
  - It lives in the upper canopy of tropical rainforests.
  - Large areas of unbroken forest provide the many fruiting trees it needs each day.
  - It swings arm-over-arm through the canopy using its tail as a fifth limb.
  - It spends most time in the highest branches where fruit is most abundant.
Q3 signals:
  - It barks as an alarm call when it spots a predator like an eagle.
  - A whinny call keeps scattered group members in contact while traveling.
  - It shakes branches and jumps up and down when approached by a threat.
  - Each individual has a unique call that others in the group recognize.
Q4 change:
  - It moves quickly to higher branches away from any ground disturbance.
  - The group splits into smaller units to forage when food is spread out.
  - It gathers back into a larger group at major fruiting trees.
  - It avoids areas near loud sounds or repeated human presence.
Q5 learn:
  - Young monkeys watch adults to learn which fruiting trees are safe to visit.
  - Infants cling to the mother for over a year while learning the canopy.
  - Juveniles practice swinging and balancing before they travel independently.
Q6 babies:
  - A female gives birth to one infant after about seven months of gestation.
  - The infant clings to the mother's belly for its first few months of life.
  - Mothers carry, nurse, and teach their young for two to three years.
  - Young spider monkeys play by swinging and chasing to build skill and strength.
Q7 limits:
  - It cannot survive without large blocks of intact rainforest with many fruiting trees.
  - Forest fragmentation forces groups into smaller patches with too little food.
  - It raises only one infant every two to three years, making recovery slow.
  - Hunting and habitat loss have made it endangered across much of its range.
Q8 teaches:
  - Finding ripe fruit across a large canopy requires memory of many trees.
  - The group spreads out to find food and comes back together to rest.
  - A monkey that eats fruit and drops seeds is helping the forest grow.
