SLUG: ring-tailed-lemur
NAME: RING-TAILED LEMUR
SCI: Lemur catta
WHERE: Madagascar — dry deciduous forest and scrubland
CONS: Endangered

Q1 sense:
  - It reads scent marks on branches to gather information about other lemurs.
  - It hears group alarm calls and responds within seconds.
  - Its eyes face forward for good depth perception while leaping.
  - It sniffs noses with group members as a form of greeting and recognition.
Q2 habitat:
  - It lives in the dry deciduous forests and scrubland of southern Madagascar.
  - It spends more time on the ground than almost any other lemur.
  - Trees provide food, sleeping sites, and escape routes from ground predators.
  - It ranges widely and crosses open ground to move between tree patches.
Q3 signals:
  - Males have scent glands on their wrists and rub them onto their striped tails.
  - They wave their scented tails at rivals in what is called a stink fight.
  - Loud group calls warn the troop of aerial predators like hawks.
  - A lower call alerts the troop to ground-level threats nearby.
Q4 change:
  - It sunbathes with arms spread wide to warm up before moving.
  - The whole troop moves together in response to a predator alarm call.
  - It retreats into tree cover when it feels exposed on open ground.
  - Females, who lead the troop, direct when and where the group moves.
Q5 learn:
  - Infants cling to the mother's belly and later ride on her back.
  - Young lemurs watch adults to learn which plants and fruits are safe to eat.
  - Scent-marking behavior begins at about six months of age, first by copying adults.
Q6 babies:
  - A female typically gives birth to one infant, sometimes twins, after about 135 days.
  - Infants are born with open eyes and can grip the mother within hours.
  - The troop protects the mother and infant during the first weeks of life.
  - Young lemurs are weaned at about five months and then follow the troop independently.
Q7 limits:
  - It needs trees large enough to sleep in safely above ground predators.
  - Deforestation in Madagascar has removed most of its dry forest habitat.
  - Habitat loss has left very little suitable forest for this species.
  - Drought years reduce fruit and leaf availability and stress the entire troop.
Q8 teaches:
  - A stink fight uses chemistry instead of teeth — information as a weapon.
  - Female leadership means group decisions are made by the most experienced members.
  - Its striped tail carries scent signals in its own social world.
