SLUG: nine-banded-armadillo
NAME: NINE-BANDED ARMADILLO
SCI: Dasypus novemcinctus
WHERE: North, Central, and South America — grasslands, forest edges, and scrubland
CONS: Least concern

Q1 sense:
  - It smells insects living up to 20 centimeters below the soil surface.
  - Its hearing is good and it uses sound to detect approaching danger.
  - It stands on its hind legs and sniffs the air for nearby threats.
  - Its eyesight is poor and works best only at very close range.
Q2 habitat:
  - It lives in grasslands, forest edges, and scrubland across the Americas.
  - Soft soil is essential — it digs burrows for sleeping and raising young.
  - It stays in areas with dense insect and invertebrate populations underground.
  - It needs access to water and avoids very cold climates.
Q3 signals:
  - Mating pairs make a soft chucking sound to communicate with each other.
  - A female's anal gland scent changes when she is ready to mate.
  - It stamps its feet and stands upright when alarmed.
  - It uses scent to recognize familiar places and navigate its home range.
Q4 change:
  - When startled, it can jump straight up into the air before running.
  - It can swim across streams by swallowing air to help it float.
  - Near humans, it shifts its activity to nighttime to avoid contact.
  - Inside a burrow it arches its back to brace and resist removal.
Q5 learn:
  - Young armadillos follow the mother and observe her foraging routes.
  - They learn which substrate types hold the most insects by following her digging pattern.
  - All four identical young develop together and learn the same home range.
Q6 babies:
  - A female always gives birth to exactly four identical young.
  - The young are born with soft, leathery shells that harden within weeks.
  - Newborns are small but have their eyes open and can walk shortly after birth.
  - Young stay in the burrow with the mother before foraging on their own.
Q7 limits:
  - Cold weather slows it sharply; it has few fat reserves for winter.
  - Hard, rocky ground prevents it from digging the burrows it needs.
  - Very loud sounds or sudden disturbances cause it to freeze or flee erratically.
  - Its startle-jump response causes many deaths near roads and traffic.
Q8 teaches:
  - A sensitive nose is more useful than eyes in its underground world.
  - Its bony shell records its growth over many years.
  - It shifts to night activity near humans, showing strong behavioral flexibility.
