SLUG: capybara
NAME: CAPYBARA
SCI: Hydrochoerus hydrochaeris
WHERE: South America — wetlands, rivers, and flooded grasslands
CONS: Common in the wild

Q1 sense:
  - Its eyes are set high on its head to spot danger from above.
  - It hears soft splashing sounds and barks from group members.
  - Its nose picks up the scent of each animal in its group.
  - It feels vibrations in the water when something moves nearby.
Q2 habitat:
  - It lives near rivers, ponds, and flooded grasslands.
  - Water lets it hide quickly when a predator comes close.
  - It grazes on grasses that grow along the water's edge.
  - Mud baths keep it cool and protect its skin from the sun.
Q3 signals:
  - It barks sharply to warn the group of a nearby threat.
  - Young ones hum and purr almost constantly to stay in contact.
  - Males rub a scent gland on their snout onto plants to mark territory.
  - A low whistle from a female signals that she is ready to mate.
Q4 change:
  - When startled, the whole group rushes into the nearest water.
  - It can hold its breath underwater for several minutes.
  - After disturbance, it becomes more active at night and more cautious during the day.
  - It stands still and alert before deciding whether to flee.
Q5 learn:
  - Young capybaras learn to graze by following adults to feeding spots.
  - They stay near the group and watch adults for cues about danger.
  - Babies hear alarm calls and quickly learn which sounds mean run.
Q6 babies:
  - A female gives birth to two to eight young at one time.
  - Babies can walk, swim, and eat grass within their first week.
  - Several females in a group share nursing duties for all the young.
  - Young stay close to adults for protection from eagles and caimans.
Q7 limits:
  - It must stay close to water, or it cannot escape large predators.
  - High heat slows it down; it rests in water or mud during hot hours.
  - Loud sudden sounds trigger the whole group to stop or scatter.
  - It cannot survive long in areas where water dries up completely.
Q8 teaches:
  - One animal watching the edge of the water protects the whole group.
  - A habitat that looks safe from above may hide danger below.
  - What keeps it calm — shade, water, quiet — tells you what it needs.
