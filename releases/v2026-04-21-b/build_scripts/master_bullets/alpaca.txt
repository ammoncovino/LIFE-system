SLUG: alpaca
NAME: ALPACA
SCI: Vicugna pacos
WHERE: Andes Mountains of South America — high grasslands and farms
CONS: Domesticated; wild ancestor (vicuña) is near threatened

Q1 sense:
  - Its large ears stand upright and turn toward new sounds.
  - Its eyes give it a wide view to spot movement across open slopes.
  - It uses its nose to recognize herd members and detect unfamiliar animals.
  - Sensitive nostrils help it identify plants before it eats them.
Q2 habitat:
  - It lives on high mountain grasslands called puna in the Andes.
  - Elevations can range from 3,000 to 5,000 meters above sea level.
  - Tough, low-growing grasses are its main food in this cold, thin-air environment.
  - It lives in herds, which provides warmth and safety on exposed slopes.
Q3 signals:
  - It hums quietly to communicate with herd members while grazing.
  - A sharp alarm call alerts the whole herd when danger is spotted.
  - It spits a mouthful of stomach contents at animals that get too close.
  - Ear position and neck posture show whether it is relaxed or tense.
Q4 change:
  - When alarmed, the whole herd groups tightly together.
  - It orients its body toward the threat and watches before moving.
  - In cold weather, the herd huddles close for shared warmth.
  - It avoids unfamiliar ground and tends to retrace familiar paths.
Q5 learn:
  - A baby alpaca stays close to its mother and copies her grazing habits.
  - Young alpacas learn which plants to eat by watching adults in the herd.
  - Crias learn herd alarm signals by responding to adult calls early in life.
Q6 babies:
  - A female gives birth to one cria after a gestation of about eleven months.
  - Crias are born standing within an hour of birth.
  - The mother and cria hum to each other constantly after birth to bond.
  - Crias nurse for six to eight months and stay near the mother.
Q7 limits:
  - High altitude cold can be dangerous for young crias if shelter is lacking.
  - Its mountain grass diet does not supply enough water on its own.
  - Predators such as pumas can separate and take a cria from the herd.
  - Sudden storms at altitude can drive the herd to lower ground quickly.
Q8 teaches:
  - Its thick coat traps air and holds warmth where temperatures drop fast.
  - Living in a herd at altitude means every animal depends on the group.
  - What looks like simple grazing is a carefully balanced search for food and safety.
