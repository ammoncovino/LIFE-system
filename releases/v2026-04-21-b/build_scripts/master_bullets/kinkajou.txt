SLUG: kinkajou
NAME: KINKAJOU
SCI: Potos flavus
WHERE: Central and South America — tropical rainforest canopy
CONS: Least concern

Q1 sense:
  - Its large eyes gather light well for moving through the canopy at night.
  - It has a keen sense of smell for finding ripe fruit in the dark.
  - Its sensitive tongue reaches into flowers and fruit to gather food.
  - It hears other kinkajous from a distance and responds to their calls.
Q2 habitat:
  - It spends almost all of its life in the rainforest canopy.
  - It sleeps during the day in a hollow tree, tree fork, or palm fronds.
  - At night it travels through the canopy, visiting fruiting trees it has visited before.
  - Its prehensile tail grips branches like a fifth limb in the dark.
Q3 signals:
  - It makes a two-part snort-weedle call when greeting a known group member.
  - It hisses and screams during aggressive encounters.
  - It marks branches with scent glands on its chest and belly.
  - Scent marking increases near large fruiting trees and sleeping sites.
Q4 change:
  - It retreats to its den and sleeps through the entire day.
  - When disturbed at night, it moves quickly to a different part of the canopy.
  - It avoids areas used by neighboring kinkajou groups.
  - Strong smells or unfamiliar scents cause it to slow down and investigate carefully.
Q5 learn:
  - Young kinkajous travel with the mother and learn the trail of familiar fruiting trees.
  - They learn which flowers and fruits to eat by observation before foraging alone.
  - Social grooming and play between group members helps young ones learn group norms.
Q6 babies:
  - A female gives birth to one young after about four months of gestation.
  - The infant is born with its eyes closed and depends fully on the mother.
  - Group members share a den and may help protect the young.
  - Young kinkajous stay with the social group for an extended period before becoming independent.
Q7 limits:
  - Daytime light is a hard limit — it cannot tolerate exposure during daylight hours.
  - Loss of large canopy trees removes both its food and its sleeping places.
  - Canopy branches are essential — without them its flexible climbing skills are useless.
  - Capture for the pet trade has reduced some local populations.
Q8 teaches:
  - It pollinates flowers by visiting them at night as it forages.
  - It knows a route through the canopy by memory, not by chance.
  - What looks like a simple night out is actually a precise, learned journey.
