SLUG: bennetts-wallaby
NAME: BENNETT'S WALLABY
SCI: Macropus rufogriseus
WHERE: Australia and Tasmania — eucalypt forests and coastal heath
CONS: Common in the wild

Q1 sense:
  - Its large ears can swivel independently to listen in two directions at once.
  - Its eyes are set wide on its head for a nearly all-around view.
  - Its nose detects the scent of predators such as dingoes.
  - Its feet feel vibrations in the ground from approaching animals.
Q2 habitat:
  - It lives in eucalypt forest with open grassy clearings nearby.
  - Forest cover provides shade and shelter during the heat of the day.
  - Open grassy areas give it places to graze at dawn and dusk.
  - It can also be found in coastal heath and scrubland.
Q3 signals:
  - It thumps its hind foot loudly on the ground to warn nearby wallabies.
  - Males make a clucking sound during courtship to approach females.
  - It uses scent glands on its chest to mark resting spots.
  - A fast, heavy hop signals alarm and causes others to scatter.
Q4 change:
  - It rests quietly in dense cover during the middle of the day.
  - If approached, it hops quickly into thick vegetation.
  - It may stand upright to get a better view of an unfamiliar sound.
  - When threatened, it cools itself by licking its forearms.
Q5 learn:
  - A joey stays in the mother's pouch and travels with her for months.
  - It watches and smells what the mother eats before it grazes alone.
  - Young wallabies learn the edges of safe territory by following adults.
Q6 babies:
  - A tiny, underdeveloped joey is born and crawls into the mother's pouch.
  - The joey stays in the pouch, nursing and growing, for about nine months.
  - The mother can pause an embryo's growth until the pouch is empty.
  - After leaving the pouch, the young wallaby still returns to nurse.
Q7 limits:
  - Hot midday temperatures force it to rest in shade and limit feeding.
  - Removal of forest cover leaves it exposed and without escape routes.
  - Dogs and foxes are major threats, especially to young joeys on the ground.
  - It needs both open grass and dense cover to survive.
Q8 teaches:
  - Its pouch carries and protects young in a way few other animals can.
  - Its ears, eyes, and legs all work together to detect danger fast.
  - Its daily routine is timed around temperature, not just hunger.
