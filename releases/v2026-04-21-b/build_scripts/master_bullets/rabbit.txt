SLUG: rabbit
NAME: RABBIT
SCI: Oryctolagus cuniculus
WHERE: Worldwide — grasslands, meadows, and woodland edges
CONS: Common in the wild

Q1 sense:
  - Its large ears can turn in different directions to pick up sounds.
  - Its eyes are placed on the sides of its head for a wide view.
  - It uses its nose constantly, sniffing for predators and familiar scents.
  - Long whiskers help it feel whether a burrow entrance is wide enough.
Q2 habitat:
  - It lives in meadows, fields, and woodland edges across many continents.
  - It digs a burrow system called a warren for sleeping and raising young.
  - Open ground nearby lets it graze on grass and low plants.
  - Soft soil is needed so it can dig and expand its tunnels.
Q3 signals:
  - It thumps its back feet hard on the ground to warn others of danger.
  - It rubs its chin on objects to leave a scent mark.
  - A white flash of its tail when it runs alerts other rabbits nearby.
  - It screams loudly only when badly frightened or injured.
Q4 change:
  - It freezes when it first senses something unusual nearby.
  - Then it bolts in a zigzag path toward the nearest cover.
  - After a scare, it returns more cautiously and keeps nose up.
  - It can shift from daytime to nighttime grazing to avoid disturbance.
Q5 learn:
  - Kits watch their mother and other rabbits to learn safe feeding spots.
  - Young rabbits follow adults before foraging on their own.
  - They learn danger signals from the group by watching who reacts first.
Q6 babies:
  - A female can give birth to five or six young at one time.
  - Newborns are hairless and blind and stay in a lined nest underground.
  - The mother visits the nest briefly at night to nurse the young.
  - Young rabbits grow quickly and leave the nest in about three weeks.
Q7 limits:
  - Open ground with no cover forces it to stay near burrow entrances.
  - A loud sound or fast movement makes it freeze or sprint for safety.
  - Very cold winters limit its food and slow its activity.
  - It must drink water or eat moist plants to avoid dehydration.
Q8 teaches:
  - A rabbit that seems calm may still be watching and smelling everything.
  - Its whole body is built for sensing trouble before it arrives.
  - Its choices about where to feed and rest are shaped by hidden risks.
