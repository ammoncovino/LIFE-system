# PART 19 — HOUSTON OPERATIONS SOP: CLARITY & AUTHORITY

This SOP governs all operational decision-making at the Houston Interactive Aquarium & Animal Preserve. It defines authority, communication, and enforcement structures. It is not a guideline. It is operational law for this location. Where applicable, these same standards extend to and align with the Austin Aquarium operational framework.

**If something contradicts this SOP, this SOP wins — unless the owner has issued a more recent written directive.**

---

## SOP Rule 1: The Owner's Voice Is Final

All operational decisions ultimately rest with the owner, Ammon Covino. When the owner speaks — in person, in writing, or through a documented directive — that is the decision. It does not require debate. It does not require consensus. It requires execution.

If a manager, a senior employee, or any other individual at this facility overrides, ignores, or delays acting on an owner directive without explicit owner permission to do so, that is a system violation. It is treated as such — immediately.

**Owner directive received = owner directive executed.**

---

## SOP Rule 2: Management Authority Is Delegated, Not Autonomous

Managers at this facility operate within authority that has been delegated by the owner. They do not hold independent operational authority.

What this means:
- Managers direct daily task execution within existing approved procedures.
- Managers enforce existing rules.
- Managers do not create new rules.
- Managers do not modify systems.
- Upper managers approve purchases within authorized limits ($350 per credit card holder). New vendors and contracts require owner approval.
- Managers do not override diet sheets.
- Managers do not approve exhibit modifications.

If a manager believes a rule needs to change or a system needs adjustment, the manager brings it to the owner. The owner decides. Until the owner decides, the existing system stands.

---

## SOP Rule 3: Staff Authority Is Execution Only

Staff execute assigned tasks within written procedures. That is the entire scope of staff authority.

Staff do not:
- Approve anything
- Create procedures
- Modify exhibits
- Change diets
- Make purchasing decisions
- Adopt software or tools
- Sign anything on behalf of the facility
- Make promises to vendors, guests, or partners

If a staff member is asked to do any of the above by anyone — including a manager — the answer is: *"I need to check with the owner first."*

This is not insubordination. This is the system.

---

## SOP Rule 4: No Verbal Approvals Without Written Follow-Up

Verbal instructions and verbal approvals exist in this facility as operational communication. They are valid for immediate action direction. They are not valid as permanent records.

Any verbal instruction or approval that affects:
- Diet or feeding
- Purchasing
- Exhibit modification
- Vendor relationships
- Staff discipline

...must be followed by written documentation within 24 hours. If it is not documented, it did not happen as a formal system change.

**"He told me to do it" is not sufficient if "he" did not document it.**

---

## SOP Rule 5: Documentation Is the Proof Standard

In all matters where documentation is required, the proof standard at this facility is written records. Verbal reports alone are not sufficient.

This applies to:
- All feedings
- All food prep area cleanings
- All exhibit anomalies (document before touching anything)
- All delivery and receiving (document all incoming supplies)
- All signage changes (document before and after)

If you are uncertain whether a situation requires documentation — document it. The cost of an unnecessary record is zero. The cost of an undocumented failure is significant.

---

## SOP Rule 6: Escalation Is Mandatory, Not Optional

When a situation exceeds the authority or capability of the person facing it, escalation is mandatory. It is not a suggestion. It is not optional if you "think you can handle it."

The escalation path is:
1. Staff → Manager
2. Manager → Owner

Situations that require immediate owner escalation (do not wait for manager intermediate step if manager is unreachable):
- Any animal health emergency
- Any guest safety incident
- Any situation involving potential legal liability
- Any media inquiry
- Any request to sign a contract or agreement
- Any situation where the system has broken down in a way that cannot be corrected at the manager level

**When in doubt about whether to escalate — escalate.**

---

## SOP Rule 7: System Changes Require Written Owner Approval

Nothing about this facility's operating system changes without written owner approval. This includes:

- Diet modifications
- Exhibit changes
- Staff scheduling structures
- Vendor relationships
- Software and technology adoption
- Pricing changes
- Education content changes
- Signage changes
- Partnership terms

If a change is needed urgently, you contact the owner and wait for written approval. "We needed to do it quickly" is not an acceptable reason for making an unauthorized system change. If speed is required, escalate faster — do not bypass the approval process.

---

## SOP Rule 8: Communication Standards

All operational communication at this facility adheres to these standards:

**Clarity:** State what you are communicating, what action is required, and by when. Do not make people decode your message.

**Channel integrity:** Use the designated communication channel for each type of communication. Do not deliver operational decisions through unofficial channels and then expect them to be treated as official.

**No gossip, no politics:** Communication about operational matters stays operational. Staff disagreements about procedures go through proper escalation channels. They do not become social conflicts played out in the facility.

**Written before binding:** Anything that binds the facility to a course of action — a commitment, an agreement, a directive that changes how we operate — is written before it is binding.

**Response expectations:** If you receive a communication that requires a response, respond. Silence is not acceptance. Silence is ambiguity. Ambiguity is a system failure.

---

## SOP Rule 9: Animal Welfare Is the Non-Negotiable Baseline

Every operational decision at this facility — scheduling, staffing, purchasing, event planning, guest programming — is evaluated against one baseline: does this protect animal welfare?

If a decision compromises animal welfare, it does not happen. There are no exceptions:
- No event scheduling that disrupts animal care operations
- No staffing reductions that leave animal care understaffed
- No purchasing decisions that compromise diet quality
- No guest programming that prioritizes entertainment over animal health

This is not sentiment. This is the operating logic of the facility. The animals are the product. The product must be protected above everything else.

**Animal welfare is not a department. It is the operating standard for every department.**

---

## SOP Rule 10: Consistency Is Accountability

The same rule applied consistently to everyone is what makes a system fair. Rules applied inconsistently create instability, resentment, and system failure.

At this facility:
- The 3-Strike Escalation Ladder applies to staff AND managers.
- The authorization rule applies to everyone — not just to people whose authority is being questioned.
- Diet protocols are followed by everyone who handles food — not just by whoever was last corrected.

Consistency is not rigidity. Consistency is accountability made visible.

**A rule that only applies to some people is not a rule. It is a suggestion. We do not operate on suggestions.**
